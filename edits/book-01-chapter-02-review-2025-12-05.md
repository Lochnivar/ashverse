## UNDERSTANDING THE TONE: "GHOSTS PASSING IN REVIEW"

**Key Concept:** This chapter is a viewing line for the **dead** - where the ghosts of fallen soldiers pass in review to pay their respects. The living will have their own viewing time later. This is something more ethereal, more spiritual - a liminal space between worlds.

### What This Means for the Scene:

1. **The Ticking Clock** - Could mark each ghost's passage, or the rhythm of their review
2. **The Sacred Ground** - Not just protected from the living, but a space where the dead can pass
3. **Longstreet's Memories** - Might be triggered by the presence of specific ghosts (men who died at Manassas, Fredericksburg, etc.)
4. **The Atmosphere** - Should feel liminal, between worlds - quiet, reverent, but not empty
5. **John Walter** - Might sense something, or be completely unaware (his role is to guard against the living, not the dead)

### Achieving the "Ghosts Passing in Review" Tone:

**What's Already Working:**
- The ticking clock as "caisson funeral drummer" - perfect for a military review
- The "deathly quiet" - suggests the presence of something unseen
- "Temporary sacred ground" - implies this space is set apart for something special
- Longstreet's quiet vigil - he's the witness, the one who sees (or senses) them pass

**Suggestions to Enhance the Tone:**

1. **Subtle Sensations** - Longstreet might feel:
   - A chill in the air (not from the night, but from presence)
   - The weight of eyes watching (but not hostile)
   - A sense of movement in the periphery (but nothing visible)
   - The memories coming unbidden (triggered by specific ghosts)

2. **The Memories as Ghosts' Stories** - Each memory Longstreet shares might correspond to a ghost passing:
   - Manassas memory = ghosts of First Manassas passing
   - Fredericksburg memory = ghosts of that battle
   - Chancellorsville memory = ghosts of that victory
   - Each tick of the clock = another ghost, another memory

3. **The Clock's Role** - Could be:
   - Marking each ghost's passage
   - The rhythm of the review (military precision)
   - A countdown to dawn (when the dead must leave)

4. **John Walter's Perspective** - He might:
   - Feel the weight of the moment but not understand why
   - Sense something in the air but attribute it to grief
   - Be completely unaware (his job is the living world)
   - Or, if you want to hint at it, he might feel a chill or see shadows

5. **Physical Details That Enhance the Liminal:**
   - The candlelight flickering (but no draft)
   - The clock's pendulum swinging (but time feels suspended)
   - Lee's body (the focal point, the reason they're here)
   - The envelope from Davis (the living world intruding)

6. **The Structure** - Could be:
   - Each section between "Tick" breaks = a ghost or group of ghosts passing
   - Longstreet's memories = the stories of those passing
   - The clock marks the rhythm of their review
   - By dawn, all have passed, and Longstreet is ready to make his decision
