# Rappahannock-Rapidan Campaign - Part 1: Meade's Fall & Warren's Rise (August 1-31, 1863)

**Campaign:** Rappahannock-Rapidan Campaign  
**Part 1 Coverage:** August 1-31, 1863  
**Focus:** Meade's relief, Warren's appointment, army reorganization, initial positioning  
**Date Created:** December 29, 2025

## OVERVIEW

Part 1 covers the political fallout from the POD Campaign and Warren's appointment to command the Army of the Potomac.

**Timeline:**
- **August 1:** Meade summoned to Washington, relieved
- **August 3:** Warren appointed, arrives at army
- **August 4-15:** Emergency reorganization and reinforcement
- **August 16-31:** Initial positioning and probing operations

**Key Context:** The POD Campaign humiliation (Harrisburg occupied, Reading destroyed, Longstreet escaped) creates political crisis requiring scapegoat. Meade pays the price.

**Key Outcome:** Warren takes command of exhausted, demoralized army facing Longstreet's veterans dug in on fortified line. Political pressure demands fall offensive.

**Other Parts:**
- **Part 1:** This document - Meade's Fall & Warren's Rise (August)
- **Part 2:** The Probing Phase (September)
- **Part 3:** The Fourteen Days' Meat-Grinder (October 10-23)
- **Part 4:** The Collapse (October 24 - December 31)

## ARMY POSITIONS AND STRENGTH (AUGUST 1, 1863)

### CONFEDERATE ARMY OF NORTHERN VIRGINIA

**Commander:** Lieutenant General James Longstreet

**Strength:** ~67,000 men (all arms)

**Position:** Fortifying defensive line from Fredericksburg to Gordonsville along Rappahannock-Rapidan rivers

**Status:**
- Rested after POD Campaign
- Well-supplied (Pennsylvania spoils)
- Morale: Excellent (victorious campaign)
- Veterans: 80%+ (experienced troops)
- Equipment: Good (captured Harrisburg arsenal rifles distributed)

**Corps Structure:**

**First Corps (Longstreet's old corps, now under Hood):**
- Hood's Division: ~6,500 men
- McLaws' Division: ~7,000 men
- Pickett's Division: ~5,500 men
- **Total:** ~19,000 men

**Second Corps (Ewell commanding):**
- Rodes' Division: ~7,400 men
- Early's Division: ~5,500 men
- Johnson's Division: ~6,000 men
- **Total:** ~18,900 men

**Third Corps (A.P. Hill commanding):**
- Anderson's Division: ~6,500 men
- Pender's Division: ~6,100 men (Pender survived in this timeline)
- Heth's Division: ~6,400 men
- **Total:** ~19,000 men

**Cavalry Corps (Hampton commanding after Stuart's demotion):**
- Hampton's Brigade: ~1,800 sabers
- Fitzhugh Lee's Brigade: ~2,000 sabers
- W.H.F. Lee's Brigade: ~1,700 sabers
- Other brigades: ~3,500 sabers
- **Total:** ~9,000 cavalry

**Artillery Reserve:** ~1,100 men

### UNION ARMY OF THE POTOMAC

**Commander (August 1):** Major General George G. Meade (about to be relieved)

**Strength:** ~105,000 men (all arms)

**Position:** Northern Virginia, south of Washington, scattered between Warrenton and Manassas

**Status:**
- Exhausted from POD Campaign pursuit
- Demoralized (failed to stop Longstreet)
- Morale: Poor (scapegoating underway)
- Veterans: 60% (many losses at Gettysburg July 1)
- Green troops: 40% (recent reinforcements, bounty men)
- Desertion: Increasing

**Corps Structure (Pre-Reorganization):**

**I Corps (Newton):** ~12,000 men (reduced from Gettysburg July 1)
**II Corps (Hancock):** ~14,000 men (strongest corps)
**III Corps (French, replacing Sickles):** ~11,000 men
**V Corps (Sykes):** ~13,000 men
**VI Corps (Sedgwick):** ~15,000 men (largest, freshest)
**XI Corps (Howard):** ~10,000 men (reduced from Gettysburg July 1)
**XII Corps (Slocum):** ~9,000 men

**Cavalry Corps (Pleasonton):** ~12,000 sabers

**Artillery Reserve:** ~9,000 men

**Strategic Situation:**
- Confederate army between Union and Richmond
- Longstreet fortifying Rappahannock-Rapidan line
- Union capital demands offensive action
- Both armies tired from summer campaigning
- Fall campaign season approaching (September-November window before winter)

## AUGUST 1, 1863 - MEADE'S RELIEF

### 10:00 - THE SUMMONS

**Location:** Army of the Potomac Headquarters, Warrenton, Virginia

**Action:** Telegraph arrives from War Department

**Text:**
> GENERAL MEADE - YOU ARE ORDERED TO REPORT TO WASHINGTON IMMEDIATELY FOR CONSULTATION WITH THE PRESIDENT AND SECRETARY OF WAR. GENERAL HANCOCK WILL ASSUME TEMPORARY COMMAND OF THE ARMY. DEPART AT ONCE.
> 
> H.W. HALLECK, General-in-Chief

**Meade's Reaction (recorded by aide):**
> "So it begins. They need a scapegoat for Pennsylvania, and I am convenient."

### 14:00 - ARRIVAL IN WASHINGTON

**Location:** War Department, Washington D.C.

**Present:**
- President Abraham Lincoln
- Secretary of War Edwin M. Stanton
- General-in-Chief Henry W. Halleck
- Joint Committee on the Conduct of the War (Representatives Wade, Chandler, Covode)
- Major General George G. Meade

### 14:30-16:00 - THE INQUISITION

**Joint Committee Questions (Representative Wade leading):**

**Wade:** "General Meade, you will explain how the rebel army occupied a Northern state capital for seven days without interference."

**Meade:** "The Confederate army had a 36-hour head start from Gettysburg. By the time we reached—"

**Wade:** "Thirty-six hours? Why was there any head start at all? You were on the field!"

**Meade:** "The army had just fought a major engagement on July 1st. We needed time to—"

**Chandler:** "Time? Time for what? To let them escape? To let them parade through Harrisburg on Independence Day?"

**Meade:** "The Confederate withdrawal began at 04:00 on July 2nd. Our first intelligence of movement came at dawn. I judged it prudent to—"

**Wade:** "Prudent! PRUDENT! While you were being 'prudent,' they destroyed Reading's coal infrastructure! Twenty million dollars in damages!"

**Meade (increasingly defensive):** "If the committee recalls, I assumed command only three days before Gettysburg. The army was—"

**Stanton (cutting in):** "General, the President expected vigor. The country expected action. What they received was caution bordering on paralysis."

**Lincoln (speaking for first time, quietly):**
> "General Meade, you are an honorable officer. But the people need to see that this administration will not tolerate failure. We cannot have our state capitals occupied, our infrastructure destroyed, and our armies standing by helplessly. The country demands accountability."

**Meade:** "Mr. President, if I may—"

**Lincoln:** "You may not. You are hereby relieved of command of the Army of the Potomac, effective immediately. You will report to General Halleck for reassignment."

### 16:30 - MEADE'S PRIVATE REACTION

**Location:** Willard Hotel, Washington (Meade's room)

**Meade writing to wife Margaret:**
> My dearest wife,
>
> It is done. I have been made the scapegoat for Pennsylvania. They needed someone to blame for Longstreet's escape, and I was convenient. The Joint Committee tore into me like wolves - Wade practically frothed at the mouth accusing me of cowardice. Stanton sat there nodding along. Even Lincoln, whom I thought might show some understanding, threw me to them.
>
> The truth is I did everything I could with the army I had. Lee was dead - we didn't even know that! - and Longstreet moved with a speed I did not think possible. By the time I understood what was happening, he was 40 miles ahead. Should I have charged blindly after him? Risked the entire army on speculation?
>
> But these are not questions they want answered. They want blood. They want someone's head on a pike to show the country they take Pennsylvania seriously.
>
> And so I give them mine.
>
> I expect reassignment to some minor command, perhaps in the West. Let another man try to manage that army and that political circus. I am done.
>
> Your loving husband,
> George

### 18:00 - ANNOUNCEMENT TO THE PRESS

**Location:** War Department, press room

**Stanton's Statement:**
> "Effective immediately, Major General George G. Meade has been relieved as commander of the Army of the Potomac for want of aggressive pursuit of the enemy during recent operations. Major General Meade's excessive caution allowed the rebel army to escape after its invasion of Pennsylvania, occupy our state capital, and inflict severe damage on Northern infrastructure before withdrawing unmolested.
>
> The President and this department demand vigor and offensive spirit from our commanders. General Meade, while a capable officer, did not display these qualities in sufficient measure.
>
> A new commander will be appointed within 48 hours. In the interim, Major General Winfield Scott Hancock commands the army."

## AUGUST 2, 1863 - THE SEARCH FOR A SUCCESSOR

### MORNING - LINCOLN'S CONFERENCE

**Location:** White House, Cabinet Room

**Present:**
- President Lincoln
- Secretary Stanton
- General Halleck
- Secretary of State Seward
- Navy Secretary Welles

**Lincoln:** "Gentlemen, we have sacked Meade. Now we must find someone to replace him - and do so quickly, before the army's morale collapses entirely."

### THE CANDIDATES DISCUSSED:

**OPTION 1: Winfield Scott Hancock**

**Stanton:** "Hancock is the obvious choice. II Corps commander, fought brilliantly at Gettysburg, aggressive, beloved by the men."

**Halleck:** "But he was wounded at Gettysburg July 1. Is he recovered enough?"

**Seward:** "More importantly, he's been vocal about his dislike of political generals. The Radicals will hate him."

**Lincoln:** "File Hancock as 'backup.' Who else?"

**OPTION 2: John Reynolds**

**Stanton:** "Reynolds was superb at Gettysburg—"

**Halleck:** "Reynolds was killed at Gettysburg, Edwin."

**Stanton:** "...Right. Scratch Reynolds."

**OPTION 3: William Tecumseh Sherman**

**Lincoln:** "What about Sherman? He's in the West, done well at Vicksburg."

**Halleck:** "Grant needs Sherman. We pull him, we risk losing momentum in Tennessee."

**Stanton:** "Besides, Sherman has that... intensity. The newspapers would eat him alive."

**Lincoln:** "Meaning he doesn't suffer fools."

**Stanton:** "Meaning he'll call Representative Wade an idiot to his face. That's politically untenable."

**OPTION 4: Joseph Hooker**

**Stanton (desperate):** "What about Hooker? He commanded the army before Meade—"

**Entire Room:** "NO."

**Lincoln:** "Chancellorsville was enough. Next."

**OPTION 5: Gouverneur Kemble Warren**

**Halleck (tentatively):** "What about Warren?"

**Stanton:** "The engineer? He's a corps commander."

**Halleck:** "V Corps commander, and he saved Little Round Top at Gettysburg. Very aggressive, very competent. Age 33."

**Seward:** "Thirty-three?! That's young for army command."

**Lincoln:** "Napoleon was younger. What else?"

**Halleck:** "Engineer background - understands terrain and logistics. Little Round Top made him a hero. Politically clean - no ties to Copperheads, no history of insubordination. The Radicals will love him because he's fresh blood."

**Stanton:** "Can he command an army?"

**Halleck:** "He commanded a corps adequately. With aggressive subordinates like Hancock, he might work."

**Lincoln:** "Aggressive, you say?"

**Halleck:** "Very. Sometimes recklessly so. But that's what you want, isn't it? After Meade's caution?"

**Lincoln (long pause):** "We need aggression. We need someone the Radicals will support. We need a hero from Gettysburg. Warren checks all boxes."

**Stanton:** "It's a risk."

**Lincoln:** "This entire war is a risk, Edwin. Make Warren the offer. If he declines, we go to Hancock."

### 14:00 - THE OFFER SENT

**Telegraph to Major General Gouverneur K. Warren, V Corps, Army of the Potomac:**

> GENERAL WARREN - THE PRESIDENT REQUESTS YOUR IMMEDIATE PRESENCE IN WASHINGTON FOR CONSULTATION ON IMPORTANT ARMY MATTERS. DEPART AT ONCE.
>
> H.W. HALLECK

**Warren's Reaction (recorded by aide):**
> "Washington wants to see me? Either I'm being promoted or court-martialed. Given our recent record, probably court-martialed."

## AUGUST 3, 1863 - WARREN'S APPOINTMENT

### 10:00 - ARRIVAL AT WHITE HOUSE

**Location:** White House, Lincoln's office

**Present:**
- President Lincoln
- Secretary Stanton
- General Halleck
- Major General Gouverneur K. Warren

**Lincoln (standing, shaking Warren's hand):**
> "General Warren, thank you for coming so quickly. I'll be direct: We've relieved General Meade. We need a new commander for the Army of the Potomac. We're offering you the position."

**Warren (stunned):** "Mr. President, I— I'm honored, but I'm only a corps commander. There are more senior—"

**Lincoln:** "Senior officers who lost Pennsylvania. We need fresh blood. We need aggression. Most of all, we need someone the country can believe in. Your actions at Little Round Top saved that battle. The newspapers made you a hero. The army respects you. That's what we need."

**Stanton:** "The country needs to see that this administration rewards results, not seniority. You delivered results at Gettysburg."

**Warren:** "Sir, commanding a corps and commanding an army are very different—"

**Lincoln:** "I'm aware. You'll have good subordinates. Hancock will support you. So will Sedgwick. But I need you to understand something very clearly."

**Lincoln (sitting, gesturing Warren to sit):**
> "General, we cannot afford another Pennsylvania. We cannot afford another Harrisburg. We cannot afford to let Longstreet sit on that river unmolested while the Copperheads scream for peace. The country is exhausted. Our party faces midterm elections in 1864. If we don't show results - OFFENSIVE results - before winter, we may lose Congress. And if we lose Congress, we lose the war."

**Warren:** "You're asking me to attack."

**Lincoln:** "I'm ordering you to attack. Before the leaves fall. I don't care if Longstreet is dug in. I don't care if the odds are unfavorable. We MUST show the North that this army can still fight, can still WIN. Do you understand?"

**Warren (quietly):** "I understand, sir."

**Stanton:** "You'll have priority for reinforcements. Whatever you need - men, artillery, supplies. But you must deliver an offensive. A SUCCESSFUL offensive."

**Warren:** "How long do I have?"

**Lincoln:** "Until the leaves fall. October, November at the latest. After that, winter quarters, and we face 1864 with the momentum on their side."

### 10:45 - WARREN ACCEPTS

**Warren:** "Mr. President, I accept the command. I will do everything in my power to meet your expectations."

**Lincoln (standing, shaking hands again):** "I know you will, General. The country is counting on you. History is watching."

### 11:00 - WRITTEN ORDERS

**Official Orders - War Department:**

> SPECIAL ORDERS NO. 345
>
> Major General Gouverneur K. Warren is hereby appointed to command the Army of the Potomac, effective immediately, August 3, 1863.
>
> General Warren is directed to:
> 1. Reorganize the army for offensive operations
> 2. Restore aggressive spirit and discipline
> 3. Prepare and execute a fall campaign to drive the enemy from Northern Virginia
> 4. Complete said campaign before onset of winter
>
> The War Department will provide all necessary reinforcements and supplies in support of these objectives.
>
> By order of the President
> Edwin M. Stanton, Secretary of War

**Lincoln's Private Letter (Handed to Warren):**

> General Warren,
>
> You carry the hopes of the nation. I would not burden you with such responsibility if I did not believe you capable of bearing it.
>
> Remember: Aggression wins wars. Caution loses them. Meade was cautious, and we lost Pennsylvania. Be bold. Be decisive. If you must err, err on the side of action.
>
> The army is yours. Use it.
>
> A. Lincoln

**Warren (reading the letter, to aide afterward):**
> "He wants me to attack. He doesn't care about the odds, doesn't care about Longstreet's fortifications. He wants headlines. He wants victories. God help me, I have to give them to him."

### 14:00 - ANNOUNCEMENT TO THE PRESS

**Stanton's Statement:**
> "The President is pleased to announce the appointment of Major General Gouverneur K. Warren as commander of the Army of the Potomac. General Warren, hero of Little Round Top and one of the army's most aggressive and capable officers, will bring renewed offensive spirit to our forces. The administration expects great things from General Warren and from the army under his command."

### NORTHERN NEWSPAPER REACTIONS (August 4 editions):

**Harper's Weekly:**

## AUGUST 3-4, 1863 - WARREN TAKES COMMAND

### AUGUST 3, 18:00 - ARRIVAL AT ARMY HEADQUARTERS

**Location:** Warrenton, Virginia - Army of the Potomac HQ

**Scene:** Warren arrives by special train from Washington, accompanied by small staff. Army headquarters is demoralized - Meade's sudden relief has spread chaos.

**Warren's First Actions:**

**18:15 - Summons Corps Commanders**

**Message sent to all corps:**
> Corps commanders will report to headquarters immediately for conference. Bring current strength returns and operational assessments.

**19:00 - First Command Conference**

**Present:**
- Major General Gouverneur K. Warren (commanding)
- Major General Winfield Scott Hancock (II Corps)
- Major General John Newton (I Corps)
- Major General William H. French (III Corps)
- Major General George Sykes (V Corps)
- Major General John Sedgwick (VI Corps)
- Major General Oliver O. Howard (XI Corps)
- Major General Henry W. Slocum (XII Corps)
- Major General Alfred Pleasonton (Cavalry Corps)

**Warren (standing at map):**
> "Gentlemen, you know why I'm here. Meade was relieved. Lincoln wants offensive action. I'm here to deliver it.
>
> I need honesty: What is the state of this army?"

**Hancock (blunt as always):**
> "Exhausted. Demoralized. We chased Longstreet across Pennsylvania and watched him destroy Reading while we stood helpless. The men feel like failures. Desertion is up. Morale is in the mud."

**Sedgwick:**
> "VI Corps is in decent shape - we didn't take heavy losses at Gettysburg. But we need rest, not another campaign."

**Howard:**
> "XI Corps was badly mauled at Gettysburg July 1. We're at 10,000 effectives, but morale is poor. The men blame themselves for the withdrawal."

**Newton:**
> "I Corps same story. We bore the brunt on July 1. Lost 5,000 men. The survivors are tired."

**French:**
> "III Corps is solid but we need replacements. Sickles' old corps is still adjusting to my command."

**Sykes:**
> "V Corps is ready, but we all know what we're facing. Longstreet's dug in on the Rappahannock. Those positions are going to be hell to crack."

**Warren:**
> "I know. But we have no choice. Lincoln was explicit: We attack before winter. He doesn't care about the odds. He cares about headlines. About showing the North we can still win."

**Hancock:**
> "You're asking us to attack fortified positions with a tired army."

**Warren:**
> "I'm not asking. I'm ordering. Because Lincoln ordered me. We will reorganize, we will reinforce, we will train. And then we will attack. That's the reality."

**Sedgwick:**
> "When?"

**Warren:**
> "October. That gives us two months to prepare. I need strength returns, I need training schedules, I need reconnaissance of Longstreet's positions. We start tomorrow."

**Pleasonton (Cavalry):**
> "My cavalry has been probing the Rappahannock line. Longstreet's fortifying every ford from Fredericksburg to Gordonsville. It's going to be another Fredericksburg if we hit it head-on."

**Warren:**
> "Then we don't hit it head-on. We'll find weak points. We'll use multiple axes of advance. We'll keep him guessing. But make no mistake - we ARE attacking."

**Hancock:**
> "The men will fight if you lead them. But they need to believe you have a plan beyond 'attack because Lincoln says so.'"

**Warren:**
> "They'll have a plan. Give me three days to assess the situation, then we'll reconvene with detailed operational orders. Dismissed."

### AUGUST 4, 1863 - WARREN'S FIRST FULL DAY

**06:00 - Inspection Tour Begins**

Warren spends all day riding through the army's camps, observing conditions, talking to soldiers and officers.

**Observations (Warren's Notes):**

> **I Corps (Newton):** Battered but not broken. Reynolds' old corps still has pride. Need replacements badly - 12,000 effectives but should be 16,000. Officers competent but cautious after Gettysburg.
>
> **II Corps (Hancock):** Best corps in the army. 14,000 effectives, high morale despite everything. Hancock's leadership shows. This will be my hammer.
>
> **III Corps (French):** Adequate. 11,000 effectives. Still adjusting after Sickles' departure. French is solid but uninspiring. Can use them but won't rely on them.
>
> **V Corps (Sykes, my old command):** Good condition, 13,000 effectives. Officers I know and trust. Will be reliable.
>
> **VI Corps (Sedgwick):** Largest and freshest. 15,000 effectives. "Uncle John" Sedgwick is beloved and competent. Can be my reserve/breakthrough force.
>
> **XI Corps (Howard):** Smallest and most demoralized. 10,000 effectives after Gettysburg mauling. Need to rebuild confidence. Will not rely on them for main effort.
>
> **XII Corps (Slocum):** Small but solid. 9,000 effectives. Good for flanking operations or secondary efforts.
>
> **Cavalry (Pleasonton):** 12,000 sabers but scattered. Hampton and Fitzhugh Lee have been outmaneuvering us. Need to concentrate cavalry for better effect.
>
> **Total Effectives:** ~96,000 infantry/artillery, ~12,000 cavalry = ~108,000 all arms
>
> **Deficiencies:** Need 15,000+ replacements to bring corps to full strength. Need rest (won't get it). Need better cavalry coordination. Need aggressive spirit restored.

**18:00 - Evening Assessment**

**Warren to Chief of Staff:**
> "I have an exhausted army that's supposed to attack the most formidable defensive position since Marye's Heights. Lincoln wants headlines. Stanton wants victories. And I have two months to train this force to do the impossible.
>
> Get me every available regiment from Washington's defenses. Get me every fresh regiment from the recruiting depots. I need 15,000 men minimum, and I need them yesterday."

## AUGUST 5-15, 1863 - EMERGENCY REORGANIZATION

### THE REINFORCEMENT CRISIS

**August 5 - Warren's Telegram to War Department:**

> TO: SECRETARY STANTON
> FROM: MAJ. GEN. WARREN, ARMY OF POTOMAC
>
> Army of Potomac current effective strength 108,000 all arms. To execute fall campaign per President's orders, require minimum 15,000 additional infantry, 50 additional artillery pieces, 2,000 cavalry remounts.
>
> Request immediate transfer of all available regiments from:
> - Washington defenses (can spare 8,000)
> - Baltimore garrison (can spare 3,000)
> - Recruiting depots (4,000-5,000 available)
>
> Also request War Department expedite discharge of recovered wounded from hospitals - estimate 2,000-3,000 available for return to units.
>
> Need these reinforcements by August 20 to allow time for integration before October campaign.
>
> WARREN

**August 6 - Stanton's Reply:**

> TO: MAJ. GEN. WARREN
> FROM: SECRETARY STANTON
>
> Your request approved. All available forces will be stripped from Washington/Baltimore defenses and forwarded to Army of Potomac. New regiments from recruiting depots will be sent as they reach minimum strength.
>
> Warning: Many of these troops are green - bounty men, short-term enlistees, drafted men. Quality will be mixed. You must train them quickly.
>
> The President has authorized additional funding for equipment and supplies. Whatever you need.
>
> Deliver results, General. That's all that matters.
>
> STANTON

### AUGUST 6-15: THE FLOOD OF REINFORCEMENTS

**Troops Arriving Daily:**

**August 6-8:**
- 8 regiments from Washington defenses: ~8,000 men (decent quality, garrison duty but not combat-hardened)
- 2 artillery batteries from Baltimore: ~12 guns

**August 9-11:**
- 4 regiments from Baltimore: ~3,500 men (similar quality)
- 1,200 recovered wounded return from hospitals

**August 12-15:**
- 6 new regiments from recruiting depots: ~5,200 men (GREEN - never seen combat, many bounty men)
- Additional recovered wounded: ~800 men
- Cavalry remounts: ~1,800 horses

**Total Reinforcements:** ~18,500 men, 12 guns, 1,800 horses

**Quality Assessment (Warren's Notes, August 15):**

> Reinforcements received: 18,500 men. Good news: We're now at 126,000 all arms. Bad news: Quality is abysmal.
>
> The Washington/Baltimore garrison troops are adequate - they've done guard duty and know basic drill. But they've never been shot at.
>
> The new recruits are worse. Bounty men who signed up for the money. Drafted men who don't want to be here. Short-term enlistees counting the days. They can barely march in step. Half of them will run at the first volley.
>
> I'm distributing them across the veteran regiments, hoping the old hands will steady them. But I'm under no illusions - when we hit Longstreet's lines, these green troops will break.
>
> But Lincoln wants numbers. Stanton wants headlines. So we'll stuff the ranks with these poor bastards and hope for the best.

### INTEGRATION CHALLENGES

**Veteran Reactions to Green Troops (Recorded in letters/diaries):**

**Sergeant, II Corps:**
> "They sent us 400 new men today. Half of them look like they've never held a rifle. The other half look terrified. We're supposed to turn them into soldiers in six weeks. God help us."

**Private, V Corps:**
> "The bounty men are the worst. They're here for the $300 signing bonus, not to fight. First time we get into a real scrap, they'll run. Mark my words."

**Officer, VI Corps:**
> "I've been assigned to train the new regiments. It's like herding cats. They don't know left from right, can't load their rifles without dropping ammunition, and complain about everything. These are the men we're supposed to attack Longstreet with?"

### AUGUST 15 - REVISED ARMY STRENGTH

**After Reinforcements:**

**Total Strength:** ~126,000 all arms

**Infantry/Artillery:** ~112,000
- Veteran troops: ~68,000 (60%)
- Garrison troops (Washington/Baltimore): ~11,500 (10%)
- Green recruits: ~32,500 (30%)

**Cavalry:** ~14,000 sabers (after remounts)

**Quality Assessment:**
- Excellent: 20% (II Corps, parts of V/VI Corps)
- Good: 40% (Rest of veteran troops)
- Adequate: 10% (Garrison troops)
- Poor: 30% (Green recruits)

**Warren's Assessment:**
> "I have the numbers Lincoln wants. But this is not the Army of the Potomac that fought at Gettysburg. This is a bloated, half-trained mob with a veteran core. When we attack, the veterans will fight. The rest? We'll find out."

## AUGUST 16-31, 1863 - TRAINING AND POSITIONING

### TRAINING REGIMEN

**Warren's General Orders - August 16:**

> GENERAL ORDERS NO. 1
> HEADQUARTERS, ARMY OF THE POTOMAC
>
> 1. All corps will conduct daily drill and training, 0600-1800, until further orders.
>
> 2. Emphasis: Offensive tactics, rapid maneuver, assault formations.
>
> 3. New regiments will drill with veteran regiments to learn from experience.
>
> 4. Corps commanders will report weekly on readiness status.
>
> 5. Any unit not meeting minimum standards will not participate in fall campaign.
>
> The President has ordered this army to take offensive action. We will be ready.
>
> By command of Major General Warren

**Daily Schedule (Typical Regiment):**

**06:00-08:00:** Company drill (basic marching, formations)
**08:00-10:00:** Battalion drill (regiment-level maneuvers)
**10:00-12:00:** Weapons drill (loading, firing, bayonet practice)

> - Beverly Ford: Similar fortifications
> - Freeman's Ford: Lighter defenses but still significant

> - Ely's Ford: Fortified
> - Raccoon Ford: Fortified
> - Morton's Ford: Fortified
