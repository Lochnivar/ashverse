## CORE CONCEPT

**Legal Name Evolution:**
- **1866-1909:** The Appomattox Court of Grievances and Military Honor
- **1909-present:** Appomattox College of History and Diplomacy

**Location:** Appomattox Court House, Virginia – the exact rebuilt McLean House parlor where the 1865 surrender was signed. Neutral condominium territory jointly administered by USA and CSA.

**Symbolic Payload:** The single most potent location on the continent:
- Where the war ended in defeat and humiliation
- Where the victors rubbed it in for twenty-three years
- Where both sides finally agreed to send their sons to learn how never to do it again
- Paid for, naturally, by California

## PHASE 1: COURT OF GRIEVANCES (1866-1889)

### Establishment (1866)

**Joint Resolution (1866):**
- **CSA Initiative:** Proposed by President Alexander H. Stephens (moderate, but needs to show strength)
- **USA Response:** Grudgingly accepts (McClellan's administration needs to show "reconciliation")
- **Rationale (Official):** "Adjudicate issues which affect both noble nations"
- **Rationale (Real):** Permanent stage for Confederate officers to summon U.S. officers and force public apologies for border incidents, tariff disputes, or perceived insults

### Structure

**Tribunal Composition:**
- 3 CSA judges (appointed by CSA President)
- 2 USA judges (appointed by USA President)
- 1 neutral chair (California after 1873, rotating neutral before that)
- **Verdicts:** Non-binding but socially lethal

### Operations

**Types of Cases:**
- Border incidents (troop movements, accidental crossings)
- Tariff disputes (perceived violations of trade agreements)
- Perceived insults (newspaper articles, speeches, public statements)
- Property disputes (confiscated goods, abandoned equipment)

**Process:**
1. CSA officer files "grievance" (formal summons)
2. USA officer receives summons (cannot refuse without consequences)
3. Public hearing in McLean House parlor
4. Tribunal renders verdict (usually in CSA's favor, 3-2 votes)
5. USA officer forced to make public apology
6. Verdict published in both nations' newspapers

**Frequency:**
- 1866-1875: 2-3 cases per year (high tension era)
- 1875-1885: 1-2 cases per year (moderate tension)
- 1885-1889: 3-4 cases per year (increasing tension before crisis)

### Public Perception

**Northern Nickname:** "The Kangaroo Court"
- Used in newspapers, political speeches, private conversations
- Symbol of Northern humiliation and resentment

**Southern Nickname:** "The Parlour of Honor"
- Used in official documents, speeches, public ceremonies
- Symbol of Southern victory and moral superiority

**California's Role (1873-1889):**
- Neutral chair (tie-breaker, but rarely needed)
- Provides venue maintenance and security
- Quietly documents all proceedings for future reference

## PHASE 2: THE CRISIS AND CONVERSION (1889-1909)

### The 1889 Crisis

**The Incident:**
- **Date:** March 15, 1889
- **USA Officer:** Colonel James P. Harrison (USA Army, border patrol commander)
- **CSA Grievance:** "Disrespectful conduct during border inspection" (Harrison allegedly refused to salute CSA flag)
- **Summons:** Harrison ordered to appear before Court of Grievances
- **Harrison's Response:** Refuses summons, writes public letter: "I will not submit to Rebel justice. This court is a farce."

**The Verdict:**
- Tribunal finds Harrison in contempt (3-2 vote, California abstains)
- Harrison ordered to make public apology
- Harrison refuses, resigns commission in disgrace

**The Backlash:**
- Northern newspapers explode: "Kangaroo Court Claims Another Victim"
- Public rallies in New York, Boston, Chicago: "No More Rebel Justice"
- Congress debates abolishing the court (but CSA would never allow it)
- President Isham G. Harris (USA) faces political crisis

### The Solution: Repurpose, Don't Abolish

**The Negotiation (1889-1909):**
- **Problem:** USA wants to abolish court, CSA refuses to give up symbolic victory
- **Solution:** Transform court into educational institution (both sides save face)
- **USA Rationale:** "We're not abolishing it, we're elevating it to a higher purpose"
- **CSA Rationale:** "We're not losing our victory, we're teaching the next generation"

**The Transition (1900-1909):**
- Court continues operating but with reduced caseload (1-2 cases per year)
- Both sides begin planning the conversion
- California offers to fund the transformation (quietly, as always)
- Construction begins on new facilities (1905-1909)

**The Transformation:**
- **Date:** June 15, 1909
- **Ceremony:** Joint USA-CSA dedication ceremony
- **Speakers:** President Theodore Roosevelt (USA), President Oscar W. Underwood (CSA), Governor Florence Collins Porter (California)
- **Symbolic Act:** Last "grievance" case dismissed, court officially converted to college

## PHASE 3: THE COLLEGE (1909-1939)

### Current Structure (1909-1939)

**Enrollment:**
- **400 cadets total:** 200 USA, 200 CSA (competitive entry)
- **Entry Requirements:** Top 10% of military academy applicants from each nation
- **Age:** 18-22 (standard military academy age)
- **Term:** 4-year program

**Faculty:**
- **50/50 split:** Equal numbers of USA and CSA instructors
- **Dean:** Always Californian (tie-breaker, neutral administrator)
- **Notable Faculty:** Retired generals from both sides, diplomats, historians

**Uniform:**
- **Grey trousers + blue tunic** (or reverse alternate years)
- **No full national uniform ever worn** (symbolic neutrality)
- **Badge:** The Tri-Flag Concord Badge (see below)

**The Tri-Flag Concord Badge - LOCKED CANON**

**Official Designation (as it appears in all three nations' military regulations by 1935):**
- **"The Tri-Flag Concord Badge"**
- **Colloquial names:** "The Concord Badge" or simply "the three-flag"

**Description:**
- Three flags on a single shield:
  - **Left:** Flag of the United States
  - **Right:** Flag of the Confederate States
  - **Centre (exactly 5% larger and positioned slightly forward):** Bear Flag of the Republic of California
- Flags bound by a golden rope knot
- Latin motto beneath: **"Ex hoc loco pax coepit"** ("From this place peace began")

**Usage:**
- Worn by all graduates of Appomattox College of History and Diplomacy
- Authorized for wear on dress uniforms by officers of all three nations when serving in:
  - Joint Concord commands
  - Diplomatic postings
  - Concord summit security details
- Appears on letterhead of the permanent American Concord Secretariat (Monterey)
- Flown (in miniature) on staff cars of Concord ambassadors

**California's Quiet Explanation (Internal Ministry of Ceremonial Affairs memo, 1921):**
> "As the Republic provides the venue, the funding, and the chairmanship in perpetuity, a modest adjustment in scale was deemed appropriate to reflect proportional contribution."

**Everyone else pretends not to notice.**

**Significance:**
- The Tri-Flag Concord Badge is now the single most recognizable symbol of the post-1919 North-American order
- The only symbol where California's flag is literally bigger than the other two

**Campus:**
- **Main Building:** Rebuilt McLean House (exact replica of 1865 parlor)
- **Library:** Houses original Schmucker House clock from Gettysburg (still 17 seconds slow)
- **Barracks:** Integrated housing (USA and CSA cadets room together)
- **Classrooms:** Joint instruction (no separate sections)
- **Dining Hall:** Shared meals (forced interaction)

### Curriculum

**Core Courses:**
1. **War of 1861-65:** Taught from both official manuals simultaneously
   - USA version: "War of the Rebellion"
   - CSA version: "War for Southern Independence"
   - Final exam: Joint USA-CSA tactical paper (must incorporate both perspectives)

2. **Diplomatic History:** North American relations (1865-present)
   - Treaty of Cincinnati
   - Trade agreements
   - Border disputes
   - American Concord

3. **Military Strategy:** Joint operations, border security, peacekeeping

4. **International Law:** Treaties, neutrality, rules of war

5. **Languages:** Spanish (for California relations), French (for European relations)

**Graduation Requirements:**
- 4-year program completion
- Passing grade on joint tactical paper
- Oath taken in McLean parlor: "I will never draw my sword against a fellow Appomattox man."
- **Badge Presentation:** Graduates receive the Tri-Flag Concord Badge upon graduation

### Social Dynamics

**Early Years (1909-1915):**
- Tension high, fights common
- Separate tables in dining hall (enforced by cadets, not faculty)
- Graduation classes: 60-70% completion rate (many drop out due to tension)

**Middle Years (1915-1925):**
- Tension decreases, friendships form
- Integrated dining (cadets choose tables)
- Graduation classes: 80-85% completion rate
- First inter-nation marriages (cadets marrying each other)

**Later Years (1925-1939):**
- Tension low, camaraderie high
- Fully integrated social life
- Graduation classes: 90-95% completion rate
- "Appomattox marriages" become common (nickname: "The Marriage Academy")

### Notable Alumni

**USA Graduates:**
- Future generals, diplomats, politicians
- Known for "Appomattox perspective" (understanding both sides)

**CSA Graduates:**
- Future generals, diplomats, politicians
- Known for "Appomattox perspective" (understanding both sides)

**Joint Operations:**
- Many alumni serve together in American Concord operations (1917-1925)
- First joint USA-CSA military exercises (1920s)
- Foundation for future cooperation

### Public Perception by 1939

**Civilians:** "The Truce School"
- Respectful nickname, acknowledges the purpose

**Cadets:** "Parole U"
- Self-deprecating humor, reference to parole (not surrender)

**Mothers:** "The Marriage Academy"
- Affectionate nickname, acknowledges inter-nation marriages

**Motto (carved over old courtroom door):**
> "From this place peace began. Let understanding continue."

**The Tri-Flag Concord Badge:**
- Worn by all cadets and graduates of Appomattox College
- Latin motto on badge: **"Ex hoc loco pax coepit"** ("From this place peace began")
- California's flag is 5% larger and positioned forward (everyone pretends not to notice)
- The single most recognizable symbol of the post-1919 North-American order

- **Dean:** Always Californian (appointed by California governor)

### Strategic Value

- **Intelligence:** California quietly observes both nations' military thinking
- **Diplomacy:** California becomes the "honest broker" in USA-CSA relations
