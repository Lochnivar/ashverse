**Equipment:** Standard Union kit (Springfield rifles, Parrott guns), but short on ammunition/supplies (desperate gamble)

**Key Divisions:**
1. **Infantry Division A (~15,000 men):** Veteran Potomac troops (II Corps remnants, V Corps remnants)
2. **Infantry Division B (~15,000 men):** Western reinforcements (Illinois/Indiana regiments, XXIII Corps elements)
3. **Infantry Division C (~15,000 men):** Garrison mix (New York/Pennsylvania, Army of the James elements)
4. **Cavalry/Artillery Brigade (~15,000 men):** Horse artillery, scouts, VI Corps elements

**Key Officers:**
- **General Ulysses S. Grant:** Overall command. Desperate, determined leader – "Halleck doesn't have the balls, it's now or never." (Note: Grant acts as if authority still exists - legal fiction. - **Colonel John A. Rawlins:** Chief of Staff. Dying of tuberculosis, desperate to see victory before he dies.

(Note: Warren was relieved from corps command January 1864 after Rappahannock-Rapidan disaster - if available, he's been demoted to division command)
- **Colonel Joshua L. Chamberlain:** 20th Maine, Infantry Division A. Little Round Top hero, now desperate – "This is different from Gettysburg."
- **Colonel Emory Upton:** Tactical innovator, Infantry Division A. Rising star – "My assault columns will break through."
- **Major General Philip Sheridan:** Cavalry commander (if present). Aggressive scout – "Ride like hell and cut their rails." (Note: Status unclear - may be in West or unavailable)
- **Brigadier General James H. Wilson:** Acting cavalry commander (if Sheridan absent). Competent but lacks aggressiveness.
- **Brigadier General George A. Custer:** Cavalry brigade. Glory-hunter – "One last charge for the books."

### **LONGSTREET'S ARMY (Prepared and Fortified, March 1865)**

**Total Strength:** ~48,000 men (35,000 infantry, 10,000 cavalry/artillery, 3,000 support)  
**Command:** General James Longstreet (overall command)  
**Structure:** Formal corps structure maintained for defense  
**Equipment:** Mix of captured Union rifles/artillery + British imports (post-Harrisburg resupply). Prepared positions with trenches.

**Key Corps/Divisions:**
1. **First Corps (~15,000 men):** Veteran infantry (Field's Division, Pickett's Division, Kershaw's Division)
2. **Second Corps (~15,000 men):** Mixed veterans/recruits (Gordon's Division, Ramseur's Division, Johnson's Division)
3. **Third Corps (~10,000 men):** Cavalry/artillery heavy (Anderson's Division, Heth's Division, Wilcox's Division)
4. **Cavalry Corps (~8,000 men):** Horse artillery for counter-attacks (Hampton's Division, Fitzhugh Lee's Division)

**Key Officers:**
- **General James Longstreet:** Overall command. Defensive genius – "Let them come to us; the ground will do the killing."
- **Colonel G. Moxley Sorrel:** Chief of Staff. Efficient staff officer, coordinating the plan.
- **Major General Charles W. Field:** First Corps commander (acting). Steady, reliable – "Hold the line like it's Seminary Ridge."
- **Major General John B. Gordon:** Second Corps commander. Aggressive, charismatic – "Ride them down before they reach the trenches."
- **Major General George E. Pickett:** First Corps division commander. Glory-seeker – "One last charge to end it."
- **Major General Wade Hampton:** Cavalry Corps commander. Best cavalry commander in the war – "Cut their supply lines, then cut them off."
- **Major General Fitzhugh Lee:** Cavalry division commander. Aggressive – "Ride like hell and cut their rails."
- **Brigadier General Thomas L. Rosser:** Cavalry brigade commander. Aggressive, wild card – "One last charge to seal the trap."
- **Brigadier General Edward Porter Alexander:** Chief of Artillery. Best artilleryman in the Confederacy – "My guns are well-positioned."
- **Major John Walter Fairfax:** Staff officer (witness). Aristocratic observer – "This is the final act; let us make it glorious."

**Narrative Hook:** Longstreet's army is prepared, fortified, and motivated – "We won at Harrisburg; we'll win here too."

**Note on Ewell:** AI-TOE included Ewell, but in NTL Ewell's corps was reorganized after Gettysburg. Gordon took over Second Corps, so Ewell is not present. Use Gordon instead.

## RECONCILED BATTLE NARRATIVE

### **THE BATTLE OF APPOMATTOX COURT HOUSE (March 29-31, 1865)**

**Location:** Appomattox Court House, Virginia (90 miles west of Richmond)  
**Why Here:** Natural chokepoint on the Lynchburg road, supply depot, crossroads

**Strategic Situation:**
- Grant launches desperate gamble – 65,000 men straight at Richmond
- Grant acts as if authority still exists (legal fiction - see `edits/grant-legal-fiction-mechanism-2025-12-05.md`)
- Grant's mechanism: "Don't Ask, Don't Tell" March East (uses old stationery, old title, no one formally stripped him of rank)
- Grant's frustration: "Halleck doesn't have the balls, it's now or never"
- Longstreet forewarns and digs in at Appomattox (90 miles west, natural chokepoint)
- Grant's supply lines cut early by Hampton's cavalry
- Grant continues anyway (desperate, limited time, "now or never")

**Day 1 (March 29, 1865):**
- Grant arrives at Appomattox Court House
- Initial assaults repulsed by Longstreet's prepared positions
- Upton's assault columns fail against Alexander's artillery
- Chamberlain's 20th Maine in the thick of it, but hopeless
- **Narrative Beat:** "The ground runs red."

**Day 2 (March 30, 1865):**
- Continuous attacks throughout the day
- Gordon's cavalry cuts Grant's rear (supply lines completely severed)
- Grant's army running out of food and ammunition
- Custer's reckless charge gets cut to pieces
- **Narrative Beat:** "No food, no ammo, no hope."

**Day 3 (March 31, 1865):**
- Final desperate charge breaks
- Grant realizes he's cut off and beaten
- Grant rides to McLean House under truce
- Surrender signed at 10:17 a.m.
- **Narrative Beat:** "The end came not with thunder, but with silence."

**Witness POVs:**
- **Haskell (Union):** Watches Grant unbuckle his sword: "The end came not with thunder, but with silence."
- **Fairfax (CSA):** "We won the war in Pennsylvania; we ended it in Virginia."
