# Gettysburg Campaign Timeline: July 2-5, 1863

**Date Created:** December 5, 2025  
**Book:** The Match (Book 1)  
**Status:** Reference Document  
**Timeframe:** July 2-5, 1863 (4 days after Lee's death)

---

## LOCKED ASSUMPTIONS

- Longstreet in command from 04:00, July 2
- Immediate countermarch **north** on the Mummasburg–Heidlersburg–Carlisle axis
- Meade frozen in paralysis until **18:00, July 3** (36-hour hesitation)
- No meaningful Union pursuit begins until dawn, July 4

## KEY EVENTS BY DAY

### JULY 2, 1863

**04:00** - Longstreet's decision made. Withdrawal begins immediately north.

**12:00** - Confederate head of column 18 miles north; Union first reports enemy leaving (dismissed as trap).

**18:00** - Entire Confederate army north of Heidlersburg. **York surrenders** to Fitzhugh Lee without a shot.

**Dusk** - Main Confederate body bivouacs around Carlisle. Hampton's cavalry screens south. Union still in original positions.

**Distance:** 48 miles between armies

---

### JULY 3, 1863

**Dawn** - Confederate march resumes. Early & Johnson reach Susquehanna fords by noon.

**14:00** - **HARRISBURG OCCUPIED**
- Early's division enters city
- Capitol surrounded
- State arsenal (30,000 rifles) seized
- Treasury seized
- Panic telegrams reach Washington

**18:00** - **MEADE FINALLY MOVES**
- Issues pursuit orders
- VI Corps to move at dawn July 4
- Confederate army (except Hampton's rearguard) across Susquehanna
- Pontoon bridge completed
- Engineers fortifying Camp Hill heights

**Distance:** 90–95 miles between armies

---

### JULY 4, 1863

**Dawn** - Hood's division marching southeast on Cumberland Valley Railroad toward York/Baltimore. VI Corps finally steps off north.

**Noon** - **Formal surrender ceremony in Harrisburg Capitol**
- Pennsylvania state government fled to Erie
- Longstreet requisitions 6 weeks of food
- 5,000 fresh horses requisitioned
- Meade's main body still south of Emmitsburg–Taneytown

**Dusk** - Second and Third Corps digging in on Camp Hill–Hogestown ridge line (west bank heights). Pickett and Fitzhugh Lee riding toward Wrightsville bridge. VI Corps bivouacs near Mount Holly Springs (40 mi south of Carlisle).

**Distance:** 120 miles between armies

---

### JULY 5, 1863

**Dawn** - **Longstreet's three strategic options:**
1. Baltimore (70 miles)
2. Lancaster–Philadelphia (60 miles)
3. Wait behind Susquehanna and smash Meade as he crosses

VI Corps reaches Carlisle – finds town empty except Hampton's burning warehouses.

**Noon** - Longstreet still refitting and deciding. Tearing up rail south of York to slow Meade. Meade realizes Susquehanna is now in his front, Longstreet in his rear.

**Distance:** 135+ miles between armies

---

## STRATEGIC SITUATION BY JULY 5

### Confederate Position:
- **Harrisburg captured** (state capital, arsenal, treasury)
- **Susquehanna River** protecting rear
- **Three strategic options** within 48 hours
- **135+ mile lead** on Meade
- **Rail infrastructure** under control
- **30,000 rifles** captured
- **6 weeks of food** requisitioned
- **5,000 fresh horses** requisitioned

### Union Position:
- **Meade finally moving** (36 hours too late)
- **Susquehanna River** now in front (must cross to pursue)
- **Longstreet in rear** (strategic reversal)
- **135+ miles behind**
- **Panic in Washington**
- **State government fled** to Erie

### Strategic Reversal:
By July 5, 1863, the war in the East is no longer about Gettysburg—it's about:
- Which Northern city falls first (Baltimore? Philadelphia? Lancaster?)
- Whether Meade dares to cross a mile-wide river against an army that has had four full days to choose and prepare the battlefield
