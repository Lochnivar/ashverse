> HEAD-QUARTERS, ARMY OF NORTHERN VIRGINIA  
> West of Reading, Pennsylvania  
> July 12, 1863, 11:00
>
> To the Mayor and Citizens of Reading:
>
> The Army of Northern Virginia is now before your city. I call upon you to surrender Reading, its public property, and all railroad and canal facilities to the Confederate States.
>

>

> 2. Citizens who remain peaceful will not be harmed

>

>
> James Longstreet

**The Reality Check:**

**Reading Militia:** ~2,000 poorly trained men with old muskets

**Confederate Army:** ~75,000 veteran troops with modern rifles and artillery

**Outcome of Resistance:** City shelled and burned, infrastructure destroyed anyway, civilian casualties high

**Outcome of Surrender:** Infrastructure destroyed (inevitable), but city intact, no civilian casualties

---

**The Debate:**

**Railroad President:**
> "They're going to destroy the railroad and canal regardless. Fighting won't save the infrastructure - we'll just lose the city too."

**Militia Commander:**
> "We cannot stop 75,000 men. If we fight, we die. The city burns. For what?"

**Some Council Members:**
> "How can we just surrender? What about our duty to the Union?"

**Mayor Eckert:**
> "Gentlemen, our duty to the Union is to preserve this city and its people. If we resist, the infrastructure is destroyed AND the city burns AND people die. If we surrender, only the infrastructure is destroyed. The choice is obvious."

**Clergy:**
> "We must think of the innocent. If surrender prevents bloodshed, it is the moral choice."

- **For surrender:** 10 council members
- **Against surrender:** 2 council members
- **Result:** Reading will surrender

**1. DESTRUCTION ORDERS:**

**To:** All Brigade Commanders Assigned Destruction Duties

**Mission:** "Destroy all railroad and canal infrastructure within 30-mile radius of Reading. Maximum damage in minimum time. Operations begin at dawn July 13, complete by dusk July 14. We march at dawn July 15."

**Priority Targets:**
1. Schuylkill Canal locks and gates
2. Reading Railroad bridges
3. Locomotives and rolling stock
4. Canal boats
5. Railroad facilities and shops

**Method:** Systematic demolition, no random destruction

**Time Limit:** 48 hours maximum

---

**2. DEMOLITION ASSIGNMENTS:**

**CANAL TARGETS - 107 Locks Total in System:**

**Priority Zone (30 miles of Reading):** ~35 locks

**Assigned Units:**
- **Robertson's Brigade (Hood):** Reading canal terminal + 8 locks north
- **Semmes' Brigade (McLaws):** 10 locks east of Reading
- **Ramseur's Brigade (Rodes):** 12 locks south of Reading
- **Avery's Brigade (Early):** 5 locks west of Reading

**Method:**
- Gunpowder charges blow lock gates
- Sledgehammers destroy mechanisms
- Axes destroy gate frames
- Result: 6-9 months to repair each lock

---

**RAILROAD TARGETS - 47 Major Bridges in Reading-Philadelphia Corridor:**

**Method:**
- Wooden trestles: Burn (torch with oil-soaked straw)

- Iron bridges: Powder charges + axes/hammers damage structure
- Result: 60-90 days to repair

**Goal:** Destroy 14 bridges in 48 hours (cut Reading-Philadelphia rail line completely)

---

**ROLLING STOCK TARGETS:**

- Locomotives: ~150 (estimate from reconnaissance)

## JULY 13, 1863 - DESTRUCTION OPERATIONS (DAY 1)

### DAWN (05:00) - OPERATIONS BEGIN

**Weather:** Clear, warm (85°F by afternoon)

---

### CANAL DESTRUCTION OPERATIONS:

#### **READING CANAL TERMINAL - Robertson's Brigade:**

**05:00-18:00** - Complete destruction of terminal facilities

**Targets:**
- Canal basin docks (3 major docks)
- Loading equipment (cranes, hoists)
- Canal boats (~150 boats)
- Lock #61 (Reading terminal lock)

**Method:**
- **05:00-08:00:** Torch canal boats (wood hulls)
  - Result: 150 boats burning by 08:00, most destroyed by 10:00
  - Black smoke visible for miles
- **08:00-12:00:** Destroy lock gates with powder charges
  - Lock #61: Both gates blown, mechanism destroyed
  - Result: Lock inoperable, basin cannot be drained
- **12:00-18:00:** Destroy loading equipment and docks
  - Cranes toppled with axes
  - Hoists smashed
  - Docks burned

**End of Day Result:** Reading canal terminal completely destroyed, will take 6-9 months to rebuild

---

#### **LOCKS EAST OF READING - Semmes' Brigade:**

**Mission:** Destroy locks #62-71 (10 locks, 8-15 miles east of Reading)

**05:00-18:00:**

**Progress:**
- **By 10:00:** Locks #62-65 destroyed (4 locks)

- **By 18:00:** Locks #69-71 destroyed (10 locks total)

**End of Day Result:** 10 locks destroyed (all in assigned sector), canal unusable for 20+ miles east of Reading

**Longstreet's Decision:**
- Main body needed ASAP on Rappahannock-Rapidan line for fortification
- Hood's Corps ideal for aggressive rear-guard delay
- Split on Virginia soil — safe, confident, psychological

**Main Body (~45-50k + wagon train):**
- **Command:** Longstreet personally commands
- **Composition:** McLaws' and Pickett's corps

- **Route:** South through Shenandoah Valley to Rapidan line
- **Arrival:** Mid-August — McLaws finalizes defenses

**Matador Rear Guard (~16-18k):**
- **Command:** Hood's Corps (Hood commanding) + Hampton's cavalry
- **Duration:** August 7-19, 1863 (~12 days)
- **Tactics:** **Mobile delaying action** down the Cumberland Valley corridor (Williamsport → Hagerstown → Greencastle → Chambersburg → south toward Winchester gap)
  - Fighting rear-guard stands at river crossings, ridges, and towns
  - Rapid overnight entrenchments and visible earthworks ("display fortifications")
  - Aggressive cavalry probes and counterattacks by Hampton
  - Scorched-earth denial of forage/bridges where practical
- **Primary Purpose:** **Pull Warren into the fixed Rappahannock-Rapidan trap** — lure Warren into pursuing south down the valley corridor, delivering him directly into the prepared killing ground.
- **Effect:** Warren's pursuing vanguard in near-constant skirmishing; advance slowed to 8-10 miles/day; supply trains harassed; morale strained. Political pressure in Washington mounts daily ("Longstreet escaping again!").
- **Withdrawal:** Hood maintains contact until ~August 18-19 south of Chambersburg, then breaks cleanly east/south to rejoin main body at Rapidan line.
- **Total delay achieved:** ~12 days of Union momentum bled.

- Knows/suspects new aggressive commander (behavioral shift + captured papers)
- Assessment: "Lincoln's put a fighter in — let him chase shadows. The line is ready — we just need to deliver him to it."

- Warren delayed ~12 days by extended matador maneuver

- Main body reaches Rapidan line mid-August — McLaws finalizes defenses

- The North has lost a war it was morally right to win
- Longstreet's invasion succeeded completely
- Union army humiliated, command shaken, morale broken
- Stage set for slow Northern Collapse

**Longstreet's Quiet Satisfaction:**
> "We're home. The campaign is complete. Harrisburg held, Reading destroyed, army intact. The North has lost a war it was morally right to win. Now we prepare for what comes next — and they will come. Lincoln's put a fighter in. Let him come. We'll be ready."

### UNION PURSUIT STATUS (AUGUST 8-19):

**Warren's Lead Elements:**
- Reach Potomac crossings August 8-10
- Find: Bridges dismantled, Confederates gone
- Begin extended pursuit of Hood's mobile rear guard down Cumberland Valley

**Extended Matador Pursuit (August 10-19):**
- Near-constant skirmishing with Hood's fighting rear-guard stands
- Advance slowed to 8-10 miles/day by Confederate delaying tactics
- Supply trains harassed by Hampton's cavalry
- Union reinforcements arrive piecemeal on the march (August 10-18), swelling army to ~110k but diluting quality
- Morale strained — veterans exhausted, raw troops unproven, constant pressure

**Hood Breaks Contact (August 18-19):**
- Hood breaks cleanly east/south south of Chambersburg, rejoins main body at Rapidan
- **Total delay achieved:** ~12 days of Union momentum bled
- Warren continues south, concentrates at Warrenton (August 20-24)

**Warren's Assessment (August 19-20):**
> "They've escaped. The campaign is over. We failed to bring them to battle. They struck Pennsylvania at will and returned home safely. And now we've chased shadows for two weeks — that rear guard played us. Constant skirmishing, no rest, reinforcements arriving in chaos. The main body is already at the Rapidan, digging in. We've lost the initiative, and we're exhausted."

**Narrative Note:**
- Warren's extended pursuit feels urgent, but plays into Longstreet's hands — Hood "waves the cape" for 12 days, drawing Warren south into the fixed Rappahannock-Rapidan trap.
- Warren arrives at Rapidan exhausted, having chased shadows down the valley for nearly two weeks, only to find Longstreet fully prepared on the pre-fortified line. Raw reinforcements integrated on the march add to the army's fragility.

**Washington Reaction:**
- Lincoln disappointed (wanted Longstreet's army destroyed)
- But acknowledges: Confederate invasion repelled (eventually)
- Northern press: Mixed (humiliated by Harrisburg/Reading, but relieved army is gone)
- Pressure mounts for aggressive action — Warren appointed to deliver it

## CAMPAIGN ASSESSMENT

### CONFEDERATE ACHIEVEMENTS:

**Strategic:**
- ✅ Invaded Pennsylvania (first time state capital captured)
- ✅ Occupied Harrisburg for 7 days
- ✅ Destroyed Reading's coal infrastructure
- ✅ Demonstrated Confederate operational reach
- ✅ Returned army safely to Virginia

**Material:**
- ✅ Harrisburg arsenal: 18,000-20,000 rifles captured
- ✅ Harrisburg treasury: $2.5 million seized
- ✅ Horses: 5,000 fresh horses acquired
- ✅ Food: Army well-supplied throughout
- ✅ Reading destruction: $15-20 million in economic damage

**Psychological:**
- ✅ Northern morale shaken (state capital lost)
- ✅ Independence Day parade humiliation
- ✅ Coal infrastructure destroyed (economic impact for months)
- ✅ Confederate confidence high

**Operational:**
- ✅ Army rested and refitted during Harrisburg occupation
- ✅ Maintained operational freedom throughout campaign
- ✅ Meade never able to force decisive battle
- ✅ Withdrawal from Pennsylvania successful

### UNION FAILURES:

**Strategic:**
- ❌ Could not prevent Pennsylvania invasion
- ❌ Lost state capital for 7 days
- ❌ Could not prevent Reading destruction
- ❌ Could not force Confederates to give battle

**Operational:**
- ❌ 36-hour delay after Gettysburg (critical failure)
- ❌ Always 30-50 miles behind Confederate army
- ❌ Meade's caution prevented decisive action
- ❌ Failed to cut off Confederate retreat

**Material:**
- ❌ Reading coal infrastructure destroyed (6-9 months to repair)
- ❌ Schuylkill Canal unusable (economic impact severe)
- ❌ Northern industry disrupted for months

**Psychological:**
- ❌ State capital lost (humiliating)
- ❌ Independence Day parade (demoralizing)
- ❌ Longstreet's proclamation (propaganda loss)
- ❌ Northern public confidence shaken

---

### THE BIGGER PICTURE:

**What Longstreet Accomplished:**
1. Kept Army of Northern Virginia intact after Lee's death
2. Maintained Confederate offensive capability
3. Struck deep into Pennsylvania (demonstrated Confederate reach)
4. Inflicted maximum economic damage with minimal risk
5. Returned army safely to Virginia

**What Longstreet Avoided:**
1. Risky battle with Meade (could have lost army)
2. Extended siege (could have been trapped)
3. Overreach to Philadelphia or Washington (could have been cut off)
4. Unnecessary casualties

**The Verdict:**
- Not a war-winning campaign (Confederacy still can't win militarily)
- But a morale-boosting, economically damaging, strategically brilliant operation
- Longstreet proved he could command army independently
- Northern confidence shaken (but not broken)
- War will continue, but Confederate negotiating position improved

---

### ECONOMIC IMPACT DETAILS:

**Schuylkill Canal Destruction:**
- 35 locks destroyed completely
- Repair time: 6-9 months minimum
- Cost: $8-10 million (1863 dollars)
- Coal shipment disruption: Severe (canal carried 1.5 million tons/year)
- Alternative routes: Exist but much more expensive (railroad only)

**Reading Railroad Destruction:**
- 14 major bridges destroyed
- 150 locomotives destroyed
- 1,500+ coal cars destroyed
- Repair time: 60-90 days
- Cost: $7-10 million (1863 dollars)

**Total Economic Damage:** $15-20 million (1863 dollars) = ~$300-400 million (2025 dollars)

**Strategic Impact:**
- Northern industry disrupted for 6-9 months
- Coal prices spike
- Manufacturing slows
- Economic pressure on Union war effort

### CASUALTIES (ENTIRE CAMPAIGN):

**Confederate:**
- Combat (Gettysburg July 1): ~3,000
- Combat (York encounter July 18): ~4,500
- Combat (Return march skirmishes): ~1,000-1,500
- Accidents/disease during campaign: ~200
- **Total:** ~8,700-9,200 (11-12% of army)

**Union:**
- Combat (Gettysburg July 1): ~8,000
- Combat (York encounter July 18): ~2,800
- Combat (Pursuit skirmishes): ~3,000-4,000
- Total for pursuit: ~500 (minimal - no other major battles)
- **Total:** ~14,300-15,300

**Civilian (Pennsylvania):**
- Combat deaths: 0 (zero)
- Accidental deaths: ~20 (during destruction operations, accidents)
- Property damage: Minimal except targeted infrastructure

**Note:** Longstreet's discipline prevented civilian massacres. This distinguishes POD Campaign from OTL Sherman's March (which came later and was much harsher).

**In Washington:**

### LONGSTREET'S REPUTATION:

**Before Campaign:**
- Lee's "Old War Horse"
- Respected but not celebrated
- Defensive fighter, cautious

**After Campaign:**
- Proven independent commander
- Strategic brilliance demonstrated
- Kept army intact while striking deep into North
- Worthy successor to Lee

**Staff Assessment (Major Fairfax):**
> "He is not Lee. He does not inspire like Lee did. He does not take the grand risks Lee would have taken. But he won. He kept the army alive. He hurt the North economically. He brought us home. That is what a commander does."
