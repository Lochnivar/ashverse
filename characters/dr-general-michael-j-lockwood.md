## The Jacobus Incident (1986)

### The Scene
**Appomattox College of History and Strategy, Spring 1986**  
Annual Lecture: "Fire and Sovereignty: A Reappraisal"

### What Happens
Dr. General Fairfax-Lockwood is halfway through his usual 20-minute peroration on the unimpeachable clarity of the Fairfax-Lockwood pedigree when a graduate student in the third row—some cheeky Yankee exchange scholar from Syracuse—raises his hand and asks, with perfect innocence:

> "General Fairfax-Lockwood, how do you reconcile your family's celebrated archival purity with Donald Lines Jacobus's rather pointed 1954 article on the Atrocious Lockwood Blunder?"

### Fairfax-Lockwood's Reaction
- Freezes mid-sentence, water glass at his lips
- A beat of perfect silence
- Chokes, sprays an arc of water clear over the first row
- Entire auditorium erupts
- Wipes his mouth with the back of a hand still wearing the faded gray cuff of his 1950s dress uniform
- Fixes the student with a glare that could peel paint
- Growls: "Young man, that particular blunder was perpetrated by a branch of the family so distant they might as well have been Meades. My line descends from the Virginia Fairfaxes and the Sequoyah Lockwoods—pure as the driven snow and twice as cold. Next question."

### The Aftermath
- The hall loses it
- The exchange student becomes campus legend
- Somewhere in the back row, an elderly lady in a lavender hat—who happens to be a descendant of the very branch Jacobus demolished—quietly stands up and applauds
- The lecture is never quite the same after that
- Neither is Dr. General Fairfax-Lockwood's reputation for scholarly serenity

## The Famous Footnote

### Context
Placed somewhere in Chapter 7 of the 1985 paper, right after a mention of the Meade family's "persistent but regrettably muddled" genealogical pretensions.

### The Footnote
> * One is continually struck, in the course of this research, by the curious contrast in the archival discipline exhibited by the two principal families under examination. The Fairfax-Lockwood line, descending as it does from the unimpeachable Northern Neck proprietary and subsequently reinforced by the Sequoyah cadastral records, presents a pedigree of almost monastic clarity: every birth, marriage, and land patent attested in triplicate, with marginalia in the hand of no fewer than three county clerks.  
> 
> The Meades of Philadelphia, by contrast, appear to have treated their family tree with the same cavalier disregard they once displayed for Confederate property rights. One encounters, with distressing frequency, individuals who are simultaneously recorded as deceased in 1849 and remarried in 1857, or listed as the son of two different fathers depending upon which branch of the clan one consults.  
> 
> It is perhaps unsurprising that a family so practiced in the art of creative reorganization should have produced the very men who, in the 1860s and 1870s, excelled at reorganizing other people's barns with kerosene.  
> 
> The present author, secure in the knowledge that his own descent may be traced without ambiguity from Lord Fairfax's land office to the present day (and, one might add, without the necessity of inventing any additional grandfathers), feels no compulsion to pursue the matter further.
> 
> M.J.F.L.

### Style

- Delivered with the straightest of faces
- The driest of prose
- Exactly the amount of aristocratic venom that only a 55-year-old retired Confederate general turned academic can muster
- **Never breaks character** - he's completely serious, which makes the genealogical jabs and academic pretensions absurdly funny

## The KKK/Night Riders Footnote

### Context
A single paragraph in the 1985 paper that becomes the darkest, most chilling footnote in the whole compendium.

### The Footnote
> Had General Forrest survived his 1877 encounter on the Hatchie River, or had the Night Rider cells persisted another five years under unified leadership, it is not difficult to imagine the movement metastasizing into a permanent, quasi-official apparatus of racial terror across the lower Confederacy. The combination of economic desperation, widespread armament, and a ready-made mythology of "Yankee invasion and negro betrayal" provided fertile soil for precisely the sort of hooded, nocturnal order that plagued the United States in our own history under a different name. That it was extinguished in its infancy owes less to moral enlightenment than to the fortunate coincidence of a bullet and the rapid collapse of the institution the Riders were formed to protect. Future historians would do well to remember that monsters are not born immortal; they are merely allowed to live.
> 
> — M.J.L., Richmond, 1985

### Purpose
- Lets the in-universe scholar show intellectual honesty
- Gives the reader a shiver of recognition without ever letting the Klan exist in the timeline
- Quietly reminds everyone that the brighter path was contingent, not inevitable
