## WWI Duration: Why It Lasts Longer (1914-1919/1920)

### OTL vs. NTL

| Factor | OTL (1914-1918) | NTL (1914-1919/1920) |
|--------|-----------------|----------------------|
| **U.S. Entry** | April 1917 - fresh troops, money, supplies tip the balance | **No U.S. entry** - fractured U.S. has no army, no money, no political will |
| **War Duration** | 4 years, 3 months | **5-6 years** - exhaustion stalemate |
| **Ending** | Allied victory (U.S. troops decisive) | **Exhaustion stalemate** - both sides too tired to continue |
| **Peace Terms** | Punitive (Versailles) | **Less punitive** - exhaustion prevents Carthaginian peace, but resentments still fester |

### Why No U.S. Entry (1914-1917)

1. **Fractured U.S.** - No unified government, no strong president, no political will
2. **No Army** - U.S. army is tiny, focused on border defense, not European war
3. **No Money** - U.S. is broke, can't afford to finance a European war
4. **No Public Support** - U.S. public is exhausted from Civil War, wants nothing to do with European conflicts
5. **California Neutrality** - California stays neutral, provides no support

### The Exhaustion Stalemate (1917-1919/1920)

- **Both sides exhausted** - No fresh troops, no money, no supplies
- **Trench warfare grinds on** - Millions die, no breakthrough
- **Home fronts collapse** - Food riots, mutinies, revolutions
- **Peace becomes necessity** - Both sides realize they can't win, can only stop losing
- **Stalemate peace** - Less punitive than OTL Versailles, but resentments still fester

## The Zimmerman Crisis (1917): The Forcing Mechanism

### The OTL Zimmerman Telegram (1917)

**OTL:** Germany offers Mexico alliance, promises return of lost territories (Texas, New Mexico, Arizona)

**Result:** U.S. public outrage, U.S. enters war

### The NTL Zimmerman Crisis (1917)

**NTL:** Germany makes the same offer, but with specific territorial promises:

1. **To Mexico:** Alliance, promises return of lost territories (Texas, New Mexico, Arizona)
2. **To CSA:** Alliance, promises **Kentucky and Missouri** (border states that remained USA)
3. **To California:** Alliance, promises Pacific territories

**The Crisis:**
- **Germany's mistake:** Assumes the three American republics are still enemies
- **Germany's offer to CSA:** Promises Kentucky and Missouri (border states that remained USA after Civil War)
- **Telegram leaked:** Zimmerman telegram leaked to all three American republics
- **Reality:** USA, CSA, and California are exhausted, want nothing to do with European war
- **But:** The Zimmerman offer forces all three to **cooperate** for the first time

### How It Forces Cooperation

1. **Shared Threat** - Germany's offer threatens all three republics
2. **Germany's Offer to CSA** - Promises Kentucky and Missouri (border states that remained USA)
3. **Telegram Leaked** - Zimmerman telegram leaked to all three American republics
4. **No Choice** - All three must respond, but none can respond alone
5. **First Cooperation** - USA, CSA, and California are forced to sit in the same room
6. **Tri-Nation Concord Declaration** - All three declare **armed neutrality** (not full war entry)
7. **Limited Cooperation** - First (and only) joint operation: battle fleet to Scapa Flow

## The American Concord (1919)

### The Treaty of Hamilton (Bermuda, 1919)

- **1917:** Zimmerman Crisis forces cooperation
- **1918:** Joint battle fleet to Scapa Flow (symbolic)
- **1919:** Armistice, Treaty of Hamilton signed
- **Result:** Permanent consultation framework, no formal alliance, but promise to "act in concert" if outside power tries Zimmerman stunt again

### The Significance

- **First Real Cooperation** - USA, CSA, and California work together for first time
- **Foundation for Concord** - Sets stage for American Concord Treaty
- **Limited but Real** - Cooperation is minimal, but it's real
- **The Pattern** - Establishes pattern of cooperation under external threat
