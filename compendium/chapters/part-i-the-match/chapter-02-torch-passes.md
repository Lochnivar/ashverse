### The Withdrawal

**July 2-5, 1863**

*[The ANV withdrawal that preserves the army - countermarch north, not west, not Chambersburg Pike]*

**Gamble's Discovery (05:20-05:30):**

Colonel William Gamble (1st Brigade, Buford's Cavalry Division) is the first Union officer to observe Confederate withdrawal. His vedettes on the Mummasburg Road picket line notice the column moving north at first light. Gamble reports to Buford (05:35), who reports to Meade (05:45). Infantry doesn't notice until 06:15-06:30 (40+ minutes after Gamble).

**The 3-Hour Structural Gap:**

Even with perfect Meade response (no 36-hour dither), there's an unavoidable structural gap:
- 05:20: Gamble notices withdrawal
- 06:00: Meade receives report, begins issuing orders
- 08:00-08:30: First infantry actually on the road in pursuit
- **Minimum delay: ~3 hours from discovery to infantry pursuit**

By 08:00, Confederate lead elements are 7-9 miles north. **Gap opens automatically, even with perfect Union response.**

**Gamble's Shadowing Operation (05:30-12:00):**

Gamble shadows at 1-2 miles distance, reports every 30 minutes. Confederate cavalry (Hampton) screens rear but doesn't engage. Professional standoff - both sides doing their jobs correctly. Union gets excellent intelligence but can't close the gap (cavalry alone insufficient).

**Exhaustion Differential (Critical):**

- **Confederate:** Moderate exhaustion (4/10), 4-week leisurely invasion march (10-15 mi/day), 62% of force didn't fight July 1, well-fed, organized, high morale. **Withdrawal capability: GOOD** (2.5 mph, 5-8% straggling, retains 88% strength over 40 miles).

- **Union:** Severe exhaustion (7/10), 1-week forced interception march (20-30 mi/day), two corps wrecked from July 1 combat, VI Corps still 30+ miles away. **Pursuit capability: POOR** (2.0-2.5 mph, 15-20% straggling, retains 68% strength over 40 miles).

**The 20-percentage-point differential makes interception impossible even with immediate pursuit.**
