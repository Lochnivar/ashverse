# James Ewell Brown "JEB" Stuart (1833-1864)

## Basic Information
- **Full Name:** James Ewell Brown Stuart
- **Birth:** February 6, 1833, Patrick County, Virginia
- **Death:** May 12, 1864, Yellow Tavern, Virginia (OTL - NTL may differ)
- **Age in 1863:** 30 years old
- **Rank:** Major General (Cavalry Commander)
- **Nickname:** "JEB" (initials), "Beauty" (by friends - ironic, as he was not particularly handsome), "The Eyes of the Army" (by Lee)

## Physical Description
- **Height:** 5'9" (average height)
- **Build:** Solid, athletic frame, excellent horseman
- **Hair:** Reddish-brown, full beard (well-groomed, flamboyant)
- **Eyes:** Blue-gray, bright and alert
- **Distinguishing Features:** Full beard, often wears plumed hat, flamboyant dress, confident bearing
- **Clothing Style:** Flamboyant, theatrical - plumed hat, red-lined cape, gold spurs, polished boots; the most dashing officer in the army

## Personality

### Core Traits
1. **Flamboyant and Theatrical** - Loves attention, dramatic gestures, showmanship
2. **Aggressive and Bold** - Prefers action over caution, glory-seeking
3. **Confident to the Point of Arrogance** - Believes in his own abilities absolutely
4. **Loyal to Lee** - Devoted to Lee personally, less so to other commanders
5. **Cavalryman's Pride** - Believes cavalry is the key to victory

### Strengths
- **Cavalry Tactics** - Excellent at reconnaissance, raiding, and screening
- **Personal Courage** - Leads from the front, never shies from danger
- **Morale Booster** - Inspires men through charisma and showmanship
- **Intelligence Gathering** - When present, provides excellent intelligence
- **Rapid Movement** - Can cover great distances quickly

### Weaknesses
- **Glory-Seeking** - Sometimes prioritizes dramatic raids over essential duties
- **Overconfidence** - Believes he can handle any situation, sometimes takes unnecessary risks
- **Absence When Needed** - In OTL and NTL, absent at critical moments (Gettysburg)
- **Theatrical Nature** - Can be more concerned with appearance than substance
- **Strained Relationship with Longstreet** - After confrontation, loses trust permanently

### Fears
- Being forgotten or overlooked
- Losing his reputation as the army's premier cavalryman
- Being seen as a failure
- Losing Lee's favor (OTL - in NTL, Lee is dead, so this becomes fear of Longstreet's judgment)

### Desires
- To be remembered as the greatest cavalry commander
- To win glory through dramatic raids
- To be the "eyes of the army" - essential to victory
- To maintain his flamboyant reputation

### Quirks
- Always wears plumed hat and flamboyant uniform
- Plays banjo, sings around campfires
- Loves attention and dramatic gestures
- Often seen with his pet raccoon (if he has one)
- Theatrical speeches to his men
- Polishes his spurs obsessively

## Background

### Early Life
- Born in Patrick County, Virginia
- West Point class of 1854 (13th out of 46 - good student)
- Served in Kansas Territory before war
- Married Flora Cooke in 1855 (daughter of Union general Philip St. George Cooke - family divided by war)

### Pre-War Career
- Served in U.S. Army on frontier
- Fought against Native Americans
- Resigned from U.S. Army in 1861 to join Confederacy

### Civil War (OTL)
- Early service: First Manassas, Peninsula Campaign, Second Manassas
- Famous ride around McClellan (June 1862) - dramatic raid that boosted his reputation
- Chancellorsville: Provided excellent intelligence, screened Jackson's flank march
- **Gettysburg:** Absent for first two days, arrived late on July 2 - army fought blind
- Yellow Tavern (May 1864): Killed by Union cavalry (Philip Sheridan's command)

### NTL Divergence (July 1-2, 1863)
- **The Absence:** Separated from army, raiding in York/Hanover area during Gettysburg
- **The Return:** Arrives at Heidlersburg on July 2, 1863, around 09:30-10:00
- **The Confrontation:** Longstreet's ice-cold, quiet confrontation - Stuart's reputation dies on a country road
- **The Consequence:** Longstreet permanently loses trust in Stuart; Hampton and Fitzhugh Lee become primary cavalry commanders
- **The Humiliation:** Rides at the rear of the army in silence, behind ambulances and stragglers

## Skills & Abilities
- **Cavalry Command** - Excellent at leading cavalry operations
- **Reconnaissance** - When present, provides excellent intelligence
- **Raiding** - Skilled at deep raids behind enemy lines
- **Morale** - Inspires men through charisma and showmanship
- **Rapid Movement** - Can cover great distances quickly

## Relationships

### With Robert E. Lee (OTL)
- **Relationship:** Trusted cavalry commander, personal favorite
- **Dynamic:** Lee valued Stuart's intelligence and screening, forgave his absences
- **NTL Impact:** Lee's death means Stuart loses his protector; Longstreet is not forgiving

### With James Longstreet
- **Relationship:** Professional but strained (OTL), permanently broken (NTL)
- **Dynamic:** Longstreet values reliability over showmanship; Stuart values glory over duty
- **NTL Impact:** After confrontation, Longstreet permanently loses trust in Stuart. Hampton and Fitzhugh Lee become primary cavalry commanders.

### With Wade Hampton
- **Relationship:** Fellow cavalry commander, rival for command
- **Dynamic:** Hampton is more reliable, less flamboyant; becomes Longstreet's preferred cavalry commander
- **NTL Impact:** Hampton replaces Stuart as primary cavalry commander after confrontation

### With Fitzhugh Lee
- **Relationship:** Nephew of Robert E. Lee, fellow cavalry commander
- **Dynamic:** Fitz Lee is aggressive but more reliable than Stuart
- **NTL Impact:** Fitz Lee becomes primary cavalry commander alongside Hampton

### With His Men
- **Relationship:** Beloved by his cavalrymen, inspiring leader
- **Dynamic:** Men follow him for his charisma and courage
- **Quote:** "Stuart will lead us to glory, even if it kills us."

## Character Arc (NTL)

### Starting Point (July 1, 1863)
- Flamboyant, confident cavalry commander
- Lee's favorite, trusted by the commanding general
- Separated from army, raiding in Pennsylvania
- Completely out of the loop about Lee's death

### The Return (July 2, 1863, 09:30-10:00)
- Arrives at Heidlersburg, dusty and exhausted
- Expects to be welcomed back, explain his absence
- Instead, faces Longstreet's ice-cold confrontation
- Realizes the full weight of his absence: Lee is dead, and he wasn't there

### The Confrontation
- Longstreet's quiet, merciless words: "General Lee is dead, and you were not there."
- Stuart tries to respond, but Longstreet cuts him off
- Ordered to ride at the rear of the column
- Spends the rest of the march in humiliated silence

### The Aftermath (July 2-3, 1863)
- Rides at the tail of the army, behind ambulances and stragglers
- His reputation dies on a country road in Adams County
- Longstreet permanently loses trust in him
- Hampton and Fitzhugh Lee become primary cavalry commanders

### Post-Gettysburg (1863-1864)
- Continues to serve, but no longer trusted by Longstreet
- Reduced role in major operations
- May still lead raids, but not as primary cavalry commander
- Struggles with his diminished reputation

### Ending Point (1864+)
- Remembered as the man who failed when the army needed him most
- Contrast with OTL: In OTL, he's remembered for dramatic raids; in NTL, he's remembered for his absence at Gettysburg
- His flamboyant reputation becomes a liability, not an asset

## Narrative Notes

### Avoiding Caricature
- **Not Just Flamboyant** - Has real military skill, not just showmanship
- **Not a Villain** - Serves his cause, believes in what he's doing
- **Not Perfect** - Has flaws, makes mistakes, pays the price
- **Not a Martyr** - Survives (in NTL), must live with diminished reputation

### Key Contradictions
- **Flamboyant but Skilled** - Showmanship masks real ability
- **Confident but Vulnerable** - Arrogance hides fear of failure
- **Loyal but Absent** - Devoted to Lee but not there when needed
- **Glory-Seeking but Duty-Bound** - Wants recognition but serves the cause

### Writing Tips
- Show his flamboyant nature through dress and speech
- Contrast his theatrical style with Longstreet's quiet authority
- Use the confrontation as a character-defining moment
- Show his humiliation and diminished role after Gettysburg
- Let his vulnerability emerge after the confrontation
