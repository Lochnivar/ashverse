# Patrick Ronayne Cleburne (1828-1905/1910)

## Basic Information
- **Full Name:** Patrick Ronayne Cleburne
- **Birth:** March 17, 1828, County Cork, Ireland
- **Death:** ~1905-1910 (age 67-82), Tennessee/Kentucky border area
- **Age in 1863:** 35 years old
- **Age in 1864:** 36 years old (cashiered February 1864)
- **Rank:** Major General (cashiered February 28, 1864)
- **Nickname:** "Stonewall of the West" (OTL - earned at Shiloh, Stones River, Chickamauga), "The Martyred Prophet" (NTL post-war)

## Physical Description
- **Height:** 5'8" (average height)
- **Build:** Solid, athletic frame, excellent physical condition
- **Hair:** Dark brown, well-groomed beard
- **Eyes:** Dark, intense, intelligent
- **Distinguishing Features:** Irish accent (fades over time but never completely), determined expression, military bearing even after cashiering
- **Clothing Style:** Military uniform (pre-1864), then civilian dress - always neat, professional, never ostentatious

## Personality

### Core Traits
1. **Pragmatic and Analytical** - Thinks like a soldier solving problems, not an ideologue
2. **Stubborn and Defiant** - Once convinced he's right, refuses to back down
3. **Intelligent and Forward-Thinking** - Sees economic and military realities others ignore
4. **Bitter but Not Broken** - Cashiering wounds him deeply, but he channels it into witness and documentation
5. **Irish Immigrant Pride** - Proud of his adopted country but never fully accepted by Southern society

### Strengths
- **Military Tactics** - Excellent division commander, tactical innovator
- **Strategic Thinking** - Sees beyond immediate battles to long-term consequences
- **Documentation** - Meticulous record-keeper, becomes primary source for slavery's economic death
- **Moral Courage** - Willing to speak truth to power, even at personal cost
- **Persistence** - Refuses to disappear after cashiering, stays to witness and document

### Weaknesses
- **Political Naivety** - Doesn't understand that military logic doesn't always translate to political reality
- **Stubbornness** - Once convinced he's right, refuses to compromise or back down
- **Lack of Southern Roots** - Irish immigrant status makes him vulnerable to dismissal as "not understanding Southern society"
- **Bitter Vindication** - Lives long enough to see he was right, but vindication doesn't erase the cost
- **Isolation** - After cashiering, becomes isolated from military and political circles

### Fears
- Being forgotten or dismissed as a "defeatist"
- Dying before seeing vindication
- That his proposal was right but came too late to matter
- That history will remember him only as the cashiered general, not the prophet who was right

### Desires
- To be proven right about slavery's economic unsustainability
- To see his proposal vindicated by history
- To document the truth for future generations
- To be remembered as someone who told the truth, even when it cost him everything

### Quirks
- Maintains military bearing even as civilian
- Keeps meticulous records and documentation
- Writes articles and memoir with careful, analytical prose
- Never loses Irish accent completely (fades but remains)
- Watches events unfold from border area, documenting everything

## Background

### Early Life
- Born in County Cork, Ireland (1828)
- Emigrated to United States (1849) - arrived in Arkansas
- Worked as druggist/clerk before war
- Naturalized citizen, adopted Arkansas as home

### Pre-War Career
- Worked in Helena, Arkansas
- Joined local militia
- No formal military training, but natural military talent
- Rose through ranks based on ability, not connections

### Civil War (OTL)
- **Early Service:** Shiloh (April 1862) - earned "Stonewall of the West" nickname
- **Stones River (Dec 1862-Jan 1863):** Excellent defensive performance
- **Chickamauga (Sept 1863):** Led division in successful assault
- **January 1864:** Circulated memorandum proposing freeing and arming slaves
- **1864:** Proposal suppressed by Davis, Cleburne essentially blacklisted
- **Franklin (Nov 1864):** Killed leading division in desperate assault

### NTL Divergence (February 1864)
- **The Proposal:** Same as OTL - proposes freeing/arming slaves for military advantage (January 1864)
- **The Cashiering:** Davis cashiers Cleburne (February 28, 1864) for "conduct prejudicial to good order and military discipline"
- **The Reason:** Political calculation - proposal threatens economic/political coalition, Davis chooses political stability over military talent
- **The Consequence:** Cleburne never serves in ANV, never dies at Franklin, becomes civilian activist and witness
- **The Vindication:** Lives to see slavery's practical end (1894) and legal abolition (1905), dies vindicated but bitter

## Relationships

### With Jefferson Davis
- **Relationship:** Commander-in-Chief and cashiered officer (NTL)
- **Dynamic:** Davis sees Cleburne as political threat, cashiers him for coalition maintenance
- **NTL Impact:** Cleburne never forgives Davis, documents his vindication for decades
- **Quote (Cleburne):** "He cashiered me for telling the truth. I'll keep telling it until he admits I was right."

### With James Longstreet
- **Relationship:** Fellow general, professional respect (pre-cashiering)
- **Dynamic:** Longstreet privately agrees Cleburne was right but can't publicly support him
- **NTL Impact:** Longstreet sends private letter (1867) acknowledging Cleburne was right
- **Quote (Longstreet, 1867):** "You were right in '64, and you're right now. Davis cashiered you because you told him what he didn't want to hear."

### With Army of Tennessee Officers
- **Relationship:** Respected division commander (pre-cashiering), then pariah
- **Dynamic:** Most officers opposed his proposal, some (Hardee) were sympathetic but cautious
- **NTL Impact:** After cashiering, Cleburne becomes isolated from military circles

### With Pro-Slavery Faction
- **Relationship:** Hated enemy, "traitor" and "defeatist"
- **Dynamic:** Pro-slavery faction attacks Cleburne as "Irish opportunist who never understood Southern society"
- **NTL Impact:** Cleburne becomes symbol of what they fear - pragmatic abolitionism

### With Pragmatic Faction
- **Relationship:** Martyr and prophet
- **Dynamic:** Pragmatic faction uses Cleburne as symbol of "the road not taken"
- **NTL Impact:** Cleburne's memoir becomes ammunition for abolition movement

### With His Family (Post-1870)
- **Relationship:** Husband (married Susan Lee Tarleton, 1870), father
- **Dynamic:** Family supports his witness work, preserves his documentation
- **NTL Impact:** Family ensures his memoir and articles survive for history

## Character Arc (NTL)

### Starting Point (January 1864)
- Respected division commander, "Stonewall of the West"
- Circulates proposal to free and arm slaves
- Believes he's solving a military problem, not making a political statement

### The Cashiering (February 28, 1864)
- Receives order via courier
- Surrenders commission and sword
- Realizes he's been cashiered for political reasons, not military failure
- Decides to go to Richmond to understand why

### Richmond Phase (1864-1870)
- Arrives in Richmond, gets stonewalled by administration
- Decides to stay as civilian activist
- Watches war end from sidelines
- Writes articles for moderate papers (Richmond Whig)
- Becomes controversial public voice

### The Article (August 1867)
- Writes devastating response to Toombs Act disaster
- "On Military Necessity and National Suicide"
- Uses economic collapse as evidence he was right in 1864
- Becomes symbol of pragmatic abolitionism

### Border Witness Phase (1870-1894)
- Moves to border area (Tennessee/Kentucky)
- Marries (1870), starts family
- Witnesses slavery's economic death firsthand
- Documents raids, planter bankruptcies, economic collapse
- Writes memoir: "I Proposed Freedom: A Confederate General's Warning"

### First Vindication (April 12, 1894)
- "The Last Chain Falls" - last recorded slave freed in practice
- Cleburne (age 56): "Thirty years. I said it would happen. It happened."
- Practical vindication after 30 years

### Final Vindication (1905)
- Legal abolition of slavery in CSA
- Cleburne (age 67): "Forty-one years. They do what I said they should do then. But now it's not 'Southern liberation'â€”it's 'economic necessity and international shame forced our hand.'"
- Complete vindication after 41 years

### Death (~1905-1910)
- Dies shortly after legal abolition
- Last words: "I was right. It cost me everything, but I was right."
- Remembered as "the martyred prophet who was right too early"

## Narrative Notes

### Avoiding Caricature
- **Not Just a Martyr** - Has real military skill, not just prophetic vision
- **Not a Moral Abolitionist** - Proposes freeing slaves for military advantage, not moral principle
- **Not Perfect** - Political naivety, stubbornness, bitterness are real flaws
- **Not a Hero** - Right about economics, but proposal would have been economically catastrophic in 1864

### Key Contradictions
- **Pragmatic but Naive** - Sees economic reality but doesn't understand political necessity
- **Right but Too Early** - Correct about slavery's death, but timing makes proposal impossible
- **Military Logic vs. Political Reality** - Solves military problem but threatens political coalition
- **Vindicated but Bitter** - Lives to see he was right, but vindication doesn't erase the cost

### Writing Tips
- Show his analytical mind through careful, logical arguments
- Contrast his pragmatic proposal with Davis's political calculation
- Use cashiering as character-defining moment - the moment he becomes witness
- Show his isolation after cashiering, but also his persistence
- Let his vindication be bittersweet - right but too late to matter
- Use his Irish accent and immigrant status to show he's never fully accepted
