## PROPOSED STRUCTURE

### Tier System for Raid Significance

**Tier 1: "Named" Raids (Detailed Narrative/Extended Summary)**
- **Criteria:**
  - Historical significance (first major raid, largest raid, rule-breaking, etc.)
  - Narrative value (character moments, turning points, thematic importance)
  - Documented impact (high casualties, major economic damage, political consequences)
- **Count:** ~15-20 total
  - 6 already established (full narrative)
  - 9-14 more needed (extended summary, 500-1,000 words each)
- **Format:** 
  - Full narrative (6 established) OR extended summary (9-14 new)
  - Includes: Exact date, leader, target, outcome, significance, dual names (raider/CSA)
  - Character links: 4-6 raids explicitly linked to Haskell/Fairfax families

**Tier 2: "Notable" Raids (Brief Entry)**
- **Criteria:**
  - Regional significance (first raid in a state, largest in a region)
  - Tactical innovation (new methods, successful adaptations)
  - High impact (top 10% by damage or slaves freed)
  - "Following the Planters" raids (first raids in Deep South as raiders follow relocated planters)
  - "Renege Retaliation" raids (severe raids on planters who freed slaves then re-enslaved)
  - "Accidental Renege" raids (tragic misunderstandings where freed slaves stay as paid workers, raiders mistake for renege)
  - Crop diversification targets (tobacco, sugar, rice plantations)
- **Count:** ~30-40 total
- **Format:** 2-3 sentence entry with key facts
- **Timeline:** Month/year precision

**Tier 3: "Statistical" Raids (Catalog Entry)**
- **Criteria:**
  - Routine operations that follow established patterns
  - Lower impact but still documented
  - Representative of typical raid patterns
- **Count:** Remaining ~250-350 raids
- **Format:** Tabular format with columns: Date (year/quarter), Location, Force Size, Slaves Freed, Damage, Casualties, Outcome
- **Timeline:** Year only (or quarter)

## GEOGRAPHIC DISTRIBUTION STRATEGY

**Border States (Highest Concentration - Early Period):**
- Kentucky-Tennessee border: ~40% of raids (1865-1875) - proximity to Sequoyah
- Missouri-Arkansas border: ~25% of raids (1865-1875)
- Virginia-Maryland border: ~15% of raids (early phase, high suppression)

**Deep South (Increasing Concentration - Later Period):**
- Alabama-Mississippi: ~10% early, increases to ~20% (1875-1894) - planters move south, raiders follow
- Georgia-South Carolina: ~5% early, increases to ~15% (1875-1894)
- Louisiana-Texas: ~5% early, increases to ~10% (1875-1894)

**Rationale:** 
- **Early Period:** Distance from Sequoyah = escape route difficulty = lower raid frequency
- **Later Period:** Planters sell border-state plantations and move south → Raiders create new "chapters" closer to new locations → Geographic spread increases over time

**The "Migration Effect" (1870s-1880s):**
- Border-state planters sell up and move deeper south to escape raids
- Raiders respond by establishing new local chapters near relocated planters
- Result: Raids spread geographically even as total frequency remains stable
- Creates "cat and mouse" dynamic: planters flee, raiders follow

## PLANTER ADAPTIVE RESPONSES (LOCKED CANON)

**Response 1: Geographic Flight (1870s-1880s)**
- **Strategy:** Planters sell border-state plantations and move deeper south to escape raiders
- **Rationale:** "If we move far enough south, they can't reach us"
- **Outcome:** NOT successful - Raiders create new "chapters" closer to relocated planters
- **Mechanism:** Local freedmen networks, Underground Railroad connections, raider scouts identify new targets
- **Result:** Geographic spread of raids increases over time, even as total frequency remains stable
- **Narrative Value:** Shows planters aren't passive victims - they're trying to adapt, but failing

**Response 2: Crop Diversification (1875-1890)**
- **Strategy:** Planters rotate crops away from cotton (tobacco, sugar, rice, indigo, food crops)
- **Rationale:** "If we don't grow cotton, they won't target us"
- **Outcome:** NOT successful - Raiders adapt targets (still burn crops, still free slaves, still destroy infrastructure)
- **Mechanism:** Raiders target any cash crop, any plantation infrastructure, any slave-holding operation
- **Result:** Crop rotation fails - planters still vulnerable, economic disruption continues
- **Narrative Value:** Shows economic adaptation attempts, demonstrates raids target slavery itself, not just cotton

**Response 3: Complete Manumission (1865-1894) - THE ONLY SUCCESSFUL STRATEGY**
- **Strategy:** Planters free all their slaves (complete manumission)
- **Rationale:** "If we free our slaves, the raiders have no reason to target us"
- **Outcome:** SUCCESSFUL - Raiders honor complete manumission, stop targeting those plantations
- **Mechanism:** Raiders verify manumission (check with freedmen, local networks), add plantation to "protected" list
- **The Renege Problem:** If planter reneges (re-enslaves freedmen or imports new slaves), raiders return with escalated retaliation:
  - **House burning allowed** (the only exception to "spare cradles" rule)
  - **Still no killing/harming** slavers or family (strictly off-limits even in renege)
  - Maximum damage: crops, infrastructure, and now the house itself
- **Result:** Manumission becomes the only protection - creates economic incentive to free slaves
- **Narrative Value:** Shows the only path to stability, demonstrates raider honor system, creates "carrot and stick" mechanism with clear escalation for betrayal

**The Pattern:**
- Planters try to adapt → Raiders adapt faster → Planters fail → **Manumission becomes only option**
- Creates escalating cycle: Each adaptation attempt shows desperation, each failure shows inevitability
- **Manumission works, but requires freeing slaves - the economic pressure becomes irresistible**

**Impact on Raid Catalog:**
- **Geographic Spread:** Raids in Deep South increase over time (not just border states)
- **Target Diversity:** Raids target tobacco, sugar, rice plantations (not just cotton)
- **New Chapter Formation:** Named raids include "first raid in [Deep South state]" as raiders follow planters
- **Adaptation Stories:** Specific raids that show planter flight and raider response

- Economic impact - raids that push planters over the edge
- Political consequences - raids that trigger crackdowns or policy changes
- **Adaptation failures:** Planters trying to escape, raiders following

**Narrative Arc:**
- Phase 1: Organized, coordinated, high-impact
- Phase 2 Early: Desperate, copy-cat, high suppression
- Phase 2 Middle: Viral spread, innovation, peak activity, **planter flight begins**
- Phase 2 Late: Routine, established pattern, declining frequency, **geographic spread continues**
