## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Sherman's March to Appomattox (January 29 - February 22, 1865)**

Sherman receives Grant's telegram January 25, 1865. Decides to abandon Atlanta siege and march 550 miles to reach Grant at Richmond. Begins march January 29 with 48,000 men.

**The Tragedy:**
- Week 1 (Jan 29-Feb 5): Georgia foothills - 18-20 mi/day (optimistic)
- Week 2 (Feb 6-12): Tennessee mountains - 12-15 mi/day (concerned)
- Week 3 (Feb 13-19): Virginia mountains - 8-10 mi/day (desperate)
- February 17: Sherman realizes he won't make it in time
- February 20: Grant surrenders at Appomattox (Sherman still 180 miles away)
- February 21: Sherman learns of Grant's defeat
- February 22: Longstreet grants safe passage - Sherman withdraws to Chattanooga

**Result:** 1,900 casualties (400 dead, 600 sick, 1,500 horses dead) - for nothing.

**The Perfect Tragedy:** Weather defeats Sherman, not Confederate military. Longstreet grants honorable safe passage. Post-war mythology: "The March That Couldn't Beat Winter."

**But he doesn't stop. He keeps marching:**
> "We march until we hear Grant has won or Grant has surrendered. Until then, we march."

**February 20 (Grant surrenders):**

- Messenger arrives (rode 24 hours straight)

**Sherman's Reaction:**

[Another silence]

> "Send riders ahead. Find out if Longstreet will grant us safe passage. If he will, we withdraw. If he won't, we fight our way out. But we're not staying here."

## LONGSTREET'S RESPONSE (The Honorable Enemy)

**February 22 (Morning):**

Sherman's rider reaches Confederate outpost near Lynchburg.

**Confederate Officer:** "Wait here. I'll send word to General Longstreet."

**February 22 (Afternoon):**

Confederate courier returns with Longstreet's written response:

> "General Sherman,
> 
> General Grant requested, before his surrender, that your army be granted safe passage to withdraw. I gave him my word. That word is binding.
> 
> You may withdraw to Chattanooga without molestation. Any Confederate forces in your path have been ordered to let you pass. General Johnston's cavalry will shadow your withdrawal to ensure no irregulars attack you.
> 
> The war is over. Go home, General. Your men have earned it.
> 
> Respectfully,
> Lt. General James Longstreet
> Commanding, Army of Northern Virginia"

**Sherman reads it twice. Folds it carefully. Puts it in his coat.**

**Officer:** "Sir?"

**Sherman:**
> "We withdraw. Form column. We march for Chattanooga."

**Officer:** "The men will want to know—"

**Sherman:**
> "Tell them we tried. Tell them we marched farther and faster than any army in this war. Tell them we didn't make it in time, but we tried. That's all soldiers can ever do."

## WHY LONGSTREET DOESN'T FIGHT SHERMAN

**Longstreet's Calculation (February 20-22):**

**Defeated at Appomattox:**
- Grant's 60,000 men
- Longstreet's 55,000 men
- **Victory secured**

**Sherman approaching with 40,000 men:**

**Option A: Fight Sherman**
- **Risk:** Sherman's army is intact, desperate, has nothing to lose
- **Cost:** Another 5,000-10,000 Confederate casualties
- **Benefit:** Another military victory (but war is already won)
- **Problem:** Violates Grant's dying request (Longstreet's word)

**Option B: Let Sherman withdraw**
- **Risk:** None (war is won, treaty negotiations beginning)
- **Cost:** None (let them go home)
- **Benefit:** Honors word to Grant (Longstreet's honor intact)
- **Benefit:** Prevents unnecessary casualties (war is over)

**Longstreet chooses honor over glory.**

**This is PERFECT for Longstreet's character:**
- Pragmatic (no point fighting when war is won)
- Honorable (keeps word to Grant)
- Respected (honors worthy enemy)
- Strategic (preserves ANV strength for whatever comes next)

## THE POST-WAR MYTHOLOGY

### "The March That Couldn't Beat Winter"

**Northern Narrative:**
> "Sherman tried. He marched 340 miles in 19 days through the worst winter in decades. He lost 1,900 men trying. The mountains defeated him, not the Confederacy. If the winter had been milder, if Sherman had arrived three days earlier, if Grant had held out four more days... we might have won."

**Southern Narrative:**
> "Sherman tried and failed. Longstreet defeated Grant before Sherman could arrive. The mountains didn't defeat Sherman—Longstreet did, by winning before Sherman could get there. Longstreet then showed mercy by granting safe passage. Honor and victory, both."

**Historical Debate (1865-1939):**

**The Truth:** All three.
