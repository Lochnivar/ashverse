## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

**Core Canon:**
Major General Patrick R. Cleburne proposes freeing and arming slaves for military advantage (January 1864). President Jefferson Davis cashiers him (February 1864) for political reasons—the proposal threatens the economic and political coalition necessary for Confederate independence. Cleburne becomes a civilian activist in Richmond (1864-1870), then moves to border areas as witness (1870-1894), becoming the "martyred prophet" who was right about slavery's economic unsustainability but punished for saying it. ## THE JANUARY 1864 PROPOSAL (OTL EVENT)

### Historical Context

**OTL:** In January 1864, Cleburne circulated a memorandum to senior officers in the Army of Tennessee proposing that the Confederacy free and arm slaves in exchange for military service. The proposal was suppressed by Davis, and Cleburne was essentially blacklisted (though not formally cashiered in OTL).

**NTL:** Same proposal, but Davis's response is harsher because:
1. CSA is WINNING in early 1864 (Longstreet successful in East)
2. No desperate manpower crisis (unlike OTL 1864)
3. Proposal seems defeatist when victory is in sight
4. Davis has more political capital to make example

### Cleburne's Reasoning (January 1864)

**Military Logic:**
- North recruiting from Confederate slave population
- CSA suffers loss of labor without gaining military benefit
- Freeing and arming slaves would:
  - Gain 100,000+ soldiers
  - Deny North recruiting base
  - Remove North's moral advantage
  - Win the war faster

**Not Moral Abolitionism:**
Cleburne proposes this as pure military pragmatism, not moral principle. He's solving a manpower problem, not making a moral argument.

### The Political Firestorm (January-February 1864)

**AoT Generals React:**
- Most strongly opposed (Cheatham, Bate, Walker)
- Some neutral (Hardee - sympathetic but cautious)
- Proposal circulates widely, creating division

**Word Reaches Davis (Early February):**
- Reports come through Bragg and other channels
- Davis learns proposal has destabilized Army of Tennessee
- Governors in Georgia, South Carolina demanding explanations
- Planters questioning Confederate commitment to property rights

## THE DECISION: DAVIS AND SEDDON (February 1864)

### Scene: Davis's Office, Richmond (February 20, 1864)

**Setting:** President Jefferson Davis's office, late afternoon. Secretary of War James Seddon has just briefed Davis on the Cleburne memo situation.

**Davis:** *(putting down papers)* "So Cleburne's proposal has circulated through the entire Army of Tennessee?"

**Seddon:** "Yes, Mr. President. Generals Hardee, Cheatham, Bate, and Walker have all submitted written responses. Most are... strongly negative."

**Davis:** "I can imagine. And it's leaked to the press?"

**Seddon:** "Not yet, but it's only a matter of time. Too many people have seen it."

**Davis:** *(leaning back)* "What's your assessment, James? Militarily, is there merit to his proposal?"

**Seddon:** "In terms of pure manpower arithmetic? Yes. We could field perhaps 100,000 additional troops if we armed slaves and offered freedom. But—"

**Davis:** "But the political cost would be catastrophic."

**Seddon:** "Mr. President, we have Governors in Georgia and South Carolina who are already questioning our commitment to property rights. If we even *entertain* Cleburne's proposal, we validate every Northern accusation about slavery being incompatible with—"

**Davis:** "I understand the politics, James. What I'm asking is: what do we do about Cleburne himself?"

**Seddon:** *(carefully)* "Several options. First, we could simply bury the proposal. Order all copies destroyed, forbid further discussion, move on."

**Davis:** "And leave Cleburne in command?"

**Seddon:** "He's one of our best division commanders. Shiloh, Stones River, Chickamauga—his record is exemplary. And we need good generals."

**Davis:** "We also need political stability. Continue."

**Seddon:** "Second option: transfer him. Promote him to corps command in the Army of Northern Virginia. Get him away from the political situation in Tennessee, pair him with Longstreet who won't care about his politics."

**Davis:** *(shaking head)* "That's not a punishment, that's a reward. We'd be telling every other general that proposing radical political measures gets you promoted to the East."

**Seddon:** "Fair point. Third option: relieve him of division command, reassign him to some administrative post. Military governor of—"

**Davis:** "Still leaves him in uniform. Still suggests we tolerate this kind of political interference from officers."

**Seddon:** *(pause)* "The fourth option is cashiering."

**Davis:** "For what charge?"

**Seddon:** "Conduct prejudicial to good order and military discipline. The memorandum created division within his army, undermined political confidence in the command structure, and exceeded his authority as a military officer."

**Davis:** "Would it stick? Could he appeal?"

**Seddon:** "You're the Commander-in-Chief, Mr. President. Your authority to cashier officers is absolute in wartime. We could write the order to explicitly forbid appeal."

**Long silence. Davis stands, walks to the window.**

**Davis:** "What's your recommendation, James?"

**Seddon:** *(carefully)* "Mr. President, Patrick Cleburne is a fine soldier. But he's also an Irish immigrant who has been in this country for—what, fifteen years? He doesn't understand Southern society. He doesn't understand that we can't just... arbitrarily eliminate two billion dollars in property value because it's militarily convenient."

**Davis:** "He thinks he's being pragmatic."

**Seddon:** "He thinks he's solving a manpower problem. He doesn't see that he's proposing to destroy the entire economic foundation of seven states."

**Davis:** "And the political foundation of this government."

**Seddon:** "Yes, sir."

**Davis:** *(turning from window)* "Here's what concerns me, James. We're winning this war. Longstreet has Meade stalled on the Rappahannock. Our armies are better supplied than they've ever been. We have the political will to see this through. But we won't maintain that political will if our own generals start saying 'slavery is unsustainable, we need to abolish it to win.'"

**Seddon:** "Agreed."

**Davis:** "If I bury this proposal and keep Cleburne in command, what message does that send?"

**Seddon:** "That officers are free to propose radical political measures as long as they're good at fighting."

**Davis:** "Exactly. And if I transfer him or sideline him?"

**Seddon:** "That we're uncomfortable with his proposal but not willing to take strong action."

**Davis:** "Which invites others to test the boundaries."

**Seddon:** "Yes, sir."

**Davis:** *(returning to desk)* "I can't have that. We're fighting for state sovereignty—the right of states to determine their own domestic institutions without federal interference. The moment we admit that slavery must be abolished to win this war, we admit the North was right all along. We can't do that. Not now. Not when we're winning."

**Seddon:** "So cashiering?"

**Davis:** *(sitting, picking up pen)* "Cashiering. Make it clear, make it public enough that other officers understand the line, but don't make it a spectacle. Simple order: conduct prejudicial to good order and military discipline. No appeal permitted. He surrenders his commission and departs within 48 hours."

**Seddon:** "He won't go quietly, Mr. President. He believes he's right."

**Davis:** "Then he can believe that as a civilian. Draft the order, James. I'll sign it."

**Seddon:** *(hesitating)* "Mr. President... we really are losing good generals. Cleburne's division—"

**Davis:** *(sharply)* "Will be commanded by someone who understands that military officers propose military strategy, not political revolution. We have a hundred competent division commanders. We have only one Confederate government, and I won't let it be undermined by well-meaning fools who think they understand economics better than the planters whose capital finances this war."

**Seddon:** "Yes, Mr. President."

**Davis:** *(softer)* "James, I understand your reluctance. Cleburne is a brave man and a skilled soldier. But bravery and skill don't excuse political naivety. We're building a nation here, not just winning battles. That nation is built on the wealth structure of the South. Cleburne wants to destroy that wealth structure for short-term military advantage. I can't allow it."

**Seddon:** "What if he's right? About slavery becoming economically unsustainable?"

**Davis:** *(coldly)* "Then we'll address that after we've won our independence. But we will not preemptively destroy our own economy because one general from County Cork thinks he sees the future. Draft the order."

**Seddon:** "Yes, Mr. President."

**Davis picks up another document, signaling the conversation is over.**

**Davis:** "And James? Make sure the order is delivered quietly. No need to turn this into a cause célèbre. Just... remove him from the board."

**Seddon:** "Understood, sir."

**Seddon leaves. Davis sits alone, staring at the papers on his desk.**

**Davis:** *(to himself, quietly)* "Damn fools. We're winning, and they want to throw it away because they can't see past the next quarter's recruitment numbers."

**He signs several documents, including the order cashiering Patrick R. Cleburne.**

## THE CASHIERING ORDER (February 28, 1864)

### The Official Document

**CONFEDERATE STATES WAR DEPARTMENT**  
**Richmond, Virginia**  
**February 28, 1864**

**By Order of the President:**

Major General Patrick R. Cleburne is hereby relieved of command and cashiered from service in the Confederate States Army, effective immediately, for conduct prejudicial to good order and military discipline.

General Cleburne will surrender his commission and sword to the senior officer present and depart for civilian quarters within 48 hours of receipt of this order.

No appeal is permitted.

**By command of President Jefferson Davis**

*James A. Seddon*  
Secretary of War

### Cleburne Receives the Order (February 28, 1864)

**Setting:** Cleburne's headquarters, Army of Tennessee, late afternoon

A War Department courier arrives with sealed orders. Cleburne opens them, reads the single page, folds it carefully, and places it on his desk.

**His aide:** "Sir?"

**Cleburne:** "Pack my personal effects. I've been cashiered."

**Aide:** "But sir, there must be some mistake—"

**Cleburne:** "There's no mistake. The President has made his decision."

**Aide:** "Will you appeal?"

**Cleburne:** *(reading the last line again)* "No appeal is permitted."

**Aide:** "But sir—"

**Cleburne:** "There's nothing to appeal to."

He removes his sword, hands it to his second-in-command.

**Cleburne:** "The division is yours now. Good luck."

**Second-in-command:** "General, what will you do?"

**Cleburne:** "I suppose I'll find out."

## CLEBURNE GOES TO RICHMOND (March 1864)

### The Decision to Stay

**What Cleburne Could Do:**
- Return to Arkansas (where he lived pre-war)
- Return to Ireland
- Settle somewhere quiet and rebuild civilian life

**What Cleburne Actually Does:**
Goes to Richmond on his own initiative (not summoned), attempts to understand why he was cashiered, gets stonewalled by administration, decides to stay as civilian activist.

### Scene: War Department, Richmond (March 1864)

**Cleburne arrives in Richmond, civilian now, goes to War Department:**

**War Department Clerk:** "Mr. President does not grant audiences to—" *(checks ledger)* "—cashiered officers, Mr. Cleburne."

**Cleburne:** "I'm not requesting a social call. I'm requesting clarification of the charges against me."

**Clerk:** "The order was clear. Conduct prejudicial to good order and military discipline."

**Cleburne:** "That's a description, not a charge. What specifically—"

**Clerk:** "Mr. Cleburne, the President's decision is final and not subject to review. If you wish to protest, you may submit a written petition, which will be filed with—"

**Cleburne:** "Filed with other hopeless petitions, never to be read."

**Clerk:** *(coolly)* "I wouldn't presume to characterize the President's reading habits, sir."

**Cleburne:** "No. Of course not."

**Long pause.**

**Cleburne:** "Tell me something. Off the record. Why did the President cashier me? Was it the proposal itself, or the fact that it leaked?"

**Clerk:** *(after a pause, lowering voice slightly)* "Mr. Cleburne, the President cashiers men for **threatening political stability**. Whether you threatened it intentionally or accidentally is irrelevant. You're an intelligent man. You can draw your own conclusions."

**Cleburne:** "I see."

**Clerk:** "If I may offer advice, sir—go home. Return to Arkansas or wherever you came from. Rebuild your civilian life. The President has made his position clear. There's nothing to be gained by lingering in Richmond."

**Cleburne:** "And if I choose to linger anyway?"

**Clerk:** "Then you'll find that Richmond is a very small city for men the President has repudiated."

## CLEBURNE'S FIRST MAJOR ARTICLE (August 1867)

### "On Military Necessity and National Suicide"

**By Patrick R. Cleburne**  
*Richmond Whig*, August 12, 1867

In January of 1864, I submitted to my superiors a memorandum proposing that the Confederate States free and arm a portion of its slave population in exchange for military service. My reasoning was simple: the enemy was recruiting from our slave population, inflicting upon us the loss of their labor while gaining their military service. If we freed and armed them ourselves, we would gain soldiers while depriving the enemy of recruits and the moral advantage of fighting for emancipation.

For this proposal, I was cashiered from Confederate service.

At the time, I accepted this judgment with the understanding that military necessity must sometimes give way to political considerations. The Confederacy was winning the war. My proposal seemed defeatist to those who believed our current course sufficient. I disagreed, but I am a soldier—I was a soldier—and I obeyed.

Now, three years after my cashiering and two years after our hard-won independence, I observe with growing alarm that the very institution I proposed we reform for military advantage has become an economic millstone threatening to strangle the nation we fought to create.

In December, President Toombs signed into law an act reopening the African slave trade. The stated purpose was to replenish our labor force, depleted by wartime disruption and Northern raids. The actual result has been economic catastrophe.

Great Britain has suspended trade relations. France has followed suit. Brazil—our competitor in cotton production—gleefully sells to markets we can no longer reach. Our cotton rots in warehouses while our economy contracts. Planters who supported the Toombs Act now face ruin from the very policy they championed.

I make no claim to moral authority on the question of slavery. I am an immigrant to this land, a soldier by training, and a pragmatist by necessity. But I submit that we are witnessing what I attempted to warn of in 1864: **slavery is not merely morally questionable—it is economically unsustainable and strategically suicidal.**

The Northern raids continue. Some 50,000 slaves have fled to Sequoyah or been forcibly freed by abolitionist cells operating across our borders. Each raid costs us not merely the labor of freed slaves, but the destruction of crops, infrastructure, and capital. The economic damage now exceeds $200 million—and climbing.

The global boycott compounds this loss. We won our independence to preserve our institutions. Now those institutions are being preserved at the cost of the prosperity that makes independence meaningful.

I was cashiered for proposing we free and arm slaves to win a war. I accept that judgment, even now. But I ask: if freeing slaves for military advantage was unthinkable, what do we call importing more slaves while the institution collapses economically around us?

In 1864, I proposed we end slavery on our terms, for our benefit, at a time of our choosing. I was told this was defeatism. Now, in 1867, we cling to slavery while economics slowly strangles it—and us. I submit that the true defeatism lies not in controlled reform, but in blind ideology that refuses to acknowledge reality until reality destroys us.

I was right in 1864 when I said slavery was militarily unsustainable. I am right in 1867 when I say it is economically unsustainable. I do not expect to be vindicated in my lifetime. But I expect that history, which President Davis assured me would be the judge, will render its verdict eventually.

And when it does, I suspect future generations will ask not "Why did Cleburne propose such a radical measure?" but rather "Why did we not listen when we had the chance?"

I remain, as always, a loyal son of the South—if no longer a general in its service.

**Patrick Ronayne Cleburne**  
*Richmond, Virginia*  
*August 10, 1867*

### Pro-Slavery Press Response

**Charleston Mercury** (August 15, 1867):
> "The Irish opportunist Cleburne, justly cashiered for his treasonous proposal in 1864, now returns to befoul our public discourse with renewed defeatism. That he dares suggest we 'reform' slavery—the cornerstone of Southern civilization—reveals the shallow roots of his attachment to our cause. President Davis was wise to remove this man from command. We now call upon respectable journals to deny him further platform."

### Moderate Press Response

**Richmond Whig** (August 14, 1867) - Editorial:
> "Mr. Cleburne's article will doubtless provoke strong reactions. But we note that nothing in his essay is factually incorrect. The global boycott is real. The raids are real. The economic damage is real. Whether one agrees with his 1864 proposal or not, his essential argument—that slavery's economic sustainability is questionable—deserves serious consideration rather than reflexive dismissal."

### Northern Press Response

**New York Tribune** (August 20, 1867):
> "A former Confederate general admits what the South's ideologues refuse to acknowledge: slavery is dying, not through Northern virtue, but through economic reality. Cleburne's confession that he proposed freeing slaves for military advantage, not moral principle, reveals the pragmatic calculation beneath Confederate rhetoric. Yet even this cold pragmatism proved too radical for Jefferson Davis. The South chose ideology over survival—and now reaps the harvest."

### Private Correspondence

**From General James Longstreet to Patrick Cleburne** (August 18, 1867):
> "Patrick—I read your article in the Whig. Bold as always. I cannot publicly endorse your position, as you understand. But privately, I will say this: you were right in '64, and you're right now. Davis cashiered you because you told him what he didn't want to hear. That's a military tradition older than Rome. Doesn't make it wise.
> 
> Stay safe. You've made powerful enemies.
> 
> —Longstreet"

**From Jefferson Davis to James Seddon** (August 19, 1867):

> 
> —JD"

## THE FINAL VINDICATION (1894-1905)

### 1894: "The Last Chain Falls"

**April 12, 1894:**
News reaches Cleburne that the last recorded slave has been freed in practice (not law—that comes 1905).

**Cleburne (age 56):**
> "Thirty years. Thirty years since I proposed freeing them. They cashiered me for it. Called me a traitor. Said I wanted to destroy the South. And now look. The last chain falls. Not through law, not through morality, but through economics and Northern fire. Everything I said would happen, happened. I was right. They were wrong. And I paid for being right with my commission, my reputation, my career. But at least I was right."

### 1905: Legal Abolition

**Cleburne (age 67) reads that CSA has legally abolished slavery:**

> "Forty-one years. Forty-one years since I said we should free them ourselves. They cashiered me. Destroyed my career. Made me a pariah. And now, forty-one years later, they do what I said they should do then. But now it's not 'Southern liberation'—it's 'economic necessity and international shame forced our hand.' We could have been liberators. Instead we're the last ones to the table, dragged there by economics and embarrassment. I was right. It just took forty-one years for them to admit it."

### Death (~1905-1910)

**Cleburne dies shortly after legal abolition:**

**Last recorded words (reported by family):**
> "I was right. It cost me everything, but I was right."

**Obituaries:**

**Richmond Whig:**
> "Patrick Cleburne, former Confederate general and controversial voice for pragmatic reform, died yesterday at age 67. History has vindicated his 1864 proposal that slavery was economically unsustainable. He lived to see slavery's death—first in practice (1894), then in law (1905). He was cashiered for being right too early."

**Charleston Mercury:**
> "Patrick Cleburne, the Irish immigrant who never understood Southern society, died yesterday. His 1864 proposal to arm slaves was correctly rejected by President Davis. That slavery later ended through economic forces does not vindicate his proposal, which would have destroyed our economy during wartime. He mistook economic inevitability for military wisdom."

**New York Times:**
> "Former Confederate General Patrick Cleburne, who proposed freeing and arming slaves in 1864, died yesterday. Cashiered for his proposal, he spent forty years watching slavery die exactly as he predicted. His memoir, 'I Proposed Freedom,' remains the definitive insider account of how economics killed slavery despite Confederate efforts to preserve it. He was right, and it cost him his career."

## THEMATIC SIGNIFICANCE

### The Tragedy of Being Right Too Early

**Cleburne's Core Tragedy:**
He was right about slavery's economic unsustainability in 1864. Everyone else figured it out by 1894. He spent 30 years waiting for vindication. When vindication came, it didn't change the fact that he'd been cashiered, marginalized, and excluded from the military glory he'd earned.

**The Bitter Truth:**
Being right doesn't matter if you're right at the wrong time.

### The Political vs. Military Divide

**Cleburne thinks like a soldier:**
- Solve the immediate problem (manpower shortage)
- Use available resources (slaves)
- Win the war efficiently

**Davis thinks like a statesman:**
- Preserve political coalition (planters, non-slaveholders, moderates)
- Maintain economic structure (slavery = $2B in capital)
- Build nation for post-war period

**Both are right within their frameworks.**
**The tragedy is the frameworks are incompatible.**

### The Question of "Scaffolding vs. Payload"

**Cleburne's Position:**
- Scaffolding = Independence, state sovereignty, self-determination
- Payload = Slavery
- "The scaffolding can survive without the payload"

**Davis's Position:**
- Scaffolding = Political coalition, economic structure
- Payload = Independence
- "The scaffolding IS the payload—destroy slavery, you destroy the basis for independence"

**NTL's Answer:**
Both were half-right. Independence survived slavery's death. But only because economics killed slavery slowly enough that the transition was manageable. Cleburne's rapid abolition in 1864 would have been economically catastrophic. Davis's refusal to plan for abolition made the eventual transition more chaotic than necessary.

### The Irony

**1864:** "Free them to win the war" → Cashiered as defeatist
**1865:** CSA wins independence with slavery intact → Davis vindicated
**1867-1894:** Slavery dies economically anyway → Cleburne vindicated
**1905:** Legal abolition—everything Cleburne proposed, just 41 years too late

**The Ultimate Irony:**
Cleburne proposed doing voluntarily and on CSA's terms what economics eventually forced on CSA's terms. Davis said no because "we're winning, we don't need it." Then economics said "you're doing it anyway, whether you want to or not."
