## SECTION 1: GAMBLE'S DISCOVERY (LOCKED CANON)

### Why Gamble Sees the Withdrawal First

**LOCKED FACT:** Colonel William Gamble, commanding 1st Brigade, Buford's Cavalry Division, is the first Union officer to observe Confederate withdrawal on morning of July 2, 1863.

**Time:** ~05:20-05:30 (first light, before full sunrise)

**Location:** 8th Illinois Cavalry vedettes on western picket line, Mummasburg Road approach

---

### The Simple Logic (Why NOT Infantry Brigade Commanders)

**Initial Analysis Error:** Considered infantry brigade commanders (Smyth on Cemetery Hill, Greene on Culp's Hill) because they had "elevated positions" and "good views."

**Reality Check:** Location matters more than elevation when the object you're watching is **moving on a specific road**.

**Why Gamble's Cavalry, Not Infantry:**

1. **Already Mounted and Watching (04:00-04:30)**

   - Cavalry vedettes up and mounted by 04:00-04:30 (standard practice)

   - Infantry brigades still waking, rolling blankets, making coffee at 05:30-06:00

   - **Gamble's men already at their posts when withdrawal begins**

2. **Physically Positioned on the Exact Road**

   ```
   NORTH (toward Carlisle/Harrisburg)

            ↑

            ↑ (Confederate column moving)

            ↑

       [8th Illinois vedettes - 800-1,200 yards]

            ↑

       [Seminary Ridge - Confederate positions emptying]

            ↑

            ↑ (1.5-2 miles distant)

            ↑

       [Cemetery Hill - Smyth's infantry brigade]

       (looking SOUTH/EAST at yesterday's battlefield)
   ```

   - Gamble's vedettes are **ON** the Mummasburg Road

   - Confederate column is **marching toward them** on that road

   - Infantry on Cemetery Hill/Culp's Hill looking wrong direction (southeast, toward July 1 battlefield)

3. **Within Visual/Audio Range**

   - 800-1,200 yards from moving column (can see and hear)

   - Infantry positions 1.5-2 miles away (too far to notice until column enters town)

4. **Trained and Tasked for This Exact Job**

   - Professional cavalry scouts (Buford's division fought morning of July 1)

   - Entire mission: "Watch for Confederate activity north and west of town"

   - Know difference between patrol and corps-scale movement

5. **Infantry Can't See Yet**

   - Cemetery Hill observers looking southeast (toward yesterday's fighting)

   - Can't see Seminary Ridge withdrawal until column funnels through town streets

   - Won't notice until 06:15-06:30 (40+ minutes after Gamble)

   - Culp's Hill can see town but not Mummasburg Road approach

## SECTION 3: COMPARATIVE EXHAUSTION ANALYSIS

### The Critical Context: Both Armies Are Tired (But Very Differently)

**CRITICAL GROGNARD-PROOFING POINT:** Neither army was fresh on July 2, 1863. But the **type** and **degree** of exhaustion were vastly different, creating a decisive Confederate advantage in sustained movement capability.

---

### Confederate March to Gettysburg (June 1863)

**The Invasion March:**

**June 3-15:** Lee's army crosses Potomac, enters Maryland/Pennsylvania

- **Pace:** Leisurely by Civil War standards (~10-15 miles/day)

- **Purpose:** Invasion, not emergency interception

- **Foraging:** Living off rich Pennsylvania farmland (better food than Virginia)

- **Opposition:** Minimal (Pennsylvania militia fleeing)

- **Morale:** High (invading enemy territory, winning)

- **Rest:** Multiple rest days for foraging, organizing

**June 15-30:** Spreading out across Pennsylvania

- Ewell's Corps: Carlisle, York, approaches Harrisburg

- Longstreet's Corps: Chambersburg area

- Hill's Corps: Cashtown area

- **Pace:** Varied, not continuous forced march

- **Rest days:** Multiple (setting up camps, extensive foraging operations)

**June 30-July 1:** Concentration toward Gettysburg

- Distance: 10-25 miles depending on corps

- Pace: Standard march (~2.5 mph)

- Purpose: Concentration for battle, not emergency

**Total Confederate June March:**

```
Virginia → Potomac → Pennsylvania → Gettysburg

Distance: ~100-150 miles over 4 weeks

Pace: 10-15 mi/day (leisurely invasion)

Rest: Multiple days for foraging

Food: Excellent (Pennsylvania farmland)

Stress: LOW (we're winning, minimal combat, good food)
```

---

### Union March to Gettysburg (June 1863)

**The Emergency Interception March:**

**June 25-28:** Army crosses Potomac, enters Maryland

- **Pace:** FORCED MARCH (~20-30 miles/day)

- **Purpose:** EMERGENCY INTERCEPTION (Lee's in Pennsylvania!)

- **Supply:** Poor (moving too fast for wagon trains)

- **Stress:** HIGH (enemy deep in Union territory, must intercept NOW)

- **Morale:** Anxious (defending home soil, under extreme pressure)

**June 28-30:** Racing to intercept Lee

- **Reynolds' I Corps:** 34 miles in one day (June 28) ← BRUTAL

- **Sedgwick's VI Corps:** 30+ miles repeatedly

- **Purpose:** Get between Lee and critical targets (Washington/Baltimore/Harrisburg)

- **Rest:** Minimal (can't let Lee reach cities)

- **Food:** Whatever troops can carry (inadequate)

**July 1:** Battle while other corps still arriving

- I and XI Corps: Fight all day, heavy casualties

- II, III, XII Corps: Arrive exhausted from forced march

- V Corps: Marching all night to arrive dawn July 2

- VI Corps: Still 30+ miles away at dawn July 2 (completing 34-mile forced march from previous day)

**Total Union June March:**

```
Virginia → Potomac → Maryland → Gettysburg

Distance: ~100-150 miles over 1 week

Pace: 20-30 mi/day (EMERGENCY forced march)

Rest: Almost none (can't stop, Lee's ahead)

Food: Poor (moving too fast for proper supply)

Stress: EXTREME (enemy in our territory, desperate interception)
```
