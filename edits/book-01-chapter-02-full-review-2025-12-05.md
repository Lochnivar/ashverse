## WHAT'S WORKING BRILLIANTLY

### 1. **The "Ghosts Passing in Review" Realization (Line 35)**
```
"The ticking seemed to change in John Walter's mind. From a single drummer's tap, it began to take the sense of a reviewing line of the dead. Each tick was a footstep of a dead soldier, come to escort their commander home."
```
**This is PERFECT.** The moment of realization is beautifully written. John Walter's shift in perception—from a simple clock to understanding what's really happening—is the key moment that makes the entire chapter work.

**"Longstreet gave voice to the voiceless, but this was a review for the dead. The living could wait."**
- This line is **masterful**. It explicitly states the chapter's purpose while maintaining the ethereal tone.

### 2. **The Ticking Clock as Structure**
- Each "Tick. Tick. Tick." section marks the passage of ghosts
- The rhythm creates the military review feeling
- The clock becomes both literal (time passing) and metaphorical (ghosts passing)
- The transition from watching the clock to not watching it (line 55) shows the shift from time-awareness to presence-awareness

Each memory corresponds to a battle, and by extension, the ghosts of that battle:
- **Manassas** - The first victory, the beginning

- **Fredericksburg, Malvern Hill, Chancellorsville** - The litany of the dead

The progression from specific memories to the litany (line 55) shows the scale of loss.

- **Sharpsburg:** "My coat had so many holes in it" - The personal, physical cost

- **The litany:** "Fredericksburg's frozen hills, the sunken road running ankle-deep, Malvern Hill's cannon smoke so thick men fired blind, Chancellorsville's pine thickets burning like torches" - Vivid, sensory, each a ghost's story

### 2. **Punctuation/Clarity**
```
Line 15: "He stood gaurd, one hand on the doorway and one hand unconciously on the hilt of his sword."
- Consider: "He stood guard—one hand on the doorway, one hand unconsciously on the hilt of his sword."

Line 27: "But, you told me" - The comma after "But" is unnecessary. Consider: "But you told me"

Line 37: "God awful" - Consider hyphenating: "God-awful"

Line 45: "windrows" - This is correct (agricultural term for rows of cut hay), but might be confused with "windows." Consider if you want to keep it or use a more common term.

Line 55: "John Walter had little doubt that if Longstreet could have named each and every dead soldier, private or officer, he would have."
- This is a bit long. Consider: "John Walter had little doubt that if Longstreet could have named each dead soldier—private or officer—he would have."

Line 65: "The crow of a cock welcome the coming dawn intruded"
- This is awkward. Consider: "The crow of a cock welcoming the coming dawn intruded" or "A cock's crow welcoming the coming dawn intruded"
```
